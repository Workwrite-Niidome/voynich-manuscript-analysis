# Three-Vowel Language Search: Cross-Linguistic Investigation of Voynich Phonology

## The Pattern Under Investigation

The Voynich manuscript's text exhibits a 3-vowel system (a, i, o) with the following properties:
- Stems ending in **-i** categorically take suffix **-n** (100% of 69 rigid stems)
- Stems ending in **-o** predominantly take suffix **-l** (79.1% of stems)
- Stems ending in **-a** predominantly take suffix **-r** (77.1% of stems)
- Cross-word sandhi operates: the suffix choice is also influenced by the initial sound of the following word
- No known 15th-century European language matches this pattern

---

## 1. Languages with Exactly Three Vowels

### The Canonical Three-Vowel System: /a, i, u/

The minimal vowel system found in natural languages is universally /a, i, u/ (or slight variations). Languages with this system include:

| Language | Family | Region | Vowels | 15th-c. European Contact? |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Classical Arabic** | Semitic | Middle East/N. Africa | a, i, u (+ long) | **YES** - Venice traded extensively with Arabic world |
| **Central Atlas Berber (Tamazight)** | Afroasiatic | North Africa | a, i, u | Possible via Mediterranean trade |
| **Quechua** | Quechuan | South America | a, i, u | No (pre-Columbian) |
| **Aymara** | Aymaran | South America | a, i, u | No (pre-Columbian) |
| **Inuktitut / Greenlandic** | Eskimo-Aleut | Arctic | a, i, u | No |
| **Aleut** | Eskimo-Aleut | Aleutian Islands | a, i, u | No |
| **Most Australian Aboriginal** | Various | Australia | a, i, u | No |
| **Miskito** | Misumalpan | Nicaragua | a, i, u | No |
| **Cherokee** | Iroquoian | North America | a, i, u (debated) | No |
| **Haida** | Isolate | Pacific NW | a, i, u (debated) | No |

**Critical observation**: The Voynich system uses /a, i, o/ -- NOT /a, i, u/. The vowel /o/ replacing /u/ is typologically unusual for a three-vowel system. In phonological theory, three-vowel systems maximize acoustic distance by using the extreme corners of vowel space (/a, i, u/). A system with /a, i, o/ would be typologically marked and very rare among natural languages.

**However**: If Voynich "o" represents a phoneme that could be realized as [o] or [u] depending on context (allophonic variation), this becomes less unusual. Arabic /u/ is often realized as [o] in many dialects.

### Languages with Two Vowels (Vertical Vowel Systems)

Some Northwest Caucasian languages have even smaller vowel inventories:

| Language | Vowels | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Abkhaz** | /a/, /schwa/ | Surface [e], [o], [i], [u] from consonant context |
| **Ubykh** (extinct) | 2-3 vowels | 80+ consonants; vowels are contextual |
| **Kabardian** | /schwa/, /a/, /a:/ | Vertical system; surface vowels from consonant context |
| **Adyghe** | 3 vowels | 50-60 consonants |

These languages achieve rich surface vowel inventories through consonant-vowel interaction, where palatalized and labialized consonants "color" adjacent vowels. This is relevant because Voynich could theoretically have a similar system where apparent vowels are actually determined by consonant context.

### Ge'ez (Classical Ethiopic)

Contrary to initial expectation, Ge'ez has **7 vowels**, not 3. The Ge'ez script (fidel/abugida) has seven vowel orders for each consonant. However, the underlying phonological system of Proto-Ethiopic Semitic descended from Proto-Semitic's three-vowel system /a, i, u/, with additional vowels developing later.

## 2. Languages with n/l/r Sandhi Conditioned by Stem-Final Vowel

### Sanskrit External Sandhi

Sanskrit has the most elaborate documented sandhi system in any language. Key n/l/r rules:

- Final **-n** before vowels: doubles (e.g., smayan iva -> smayann iva)
- Final **-n** before tongue-tip consonants (c, ch, t, th, etc.): inserts sibilant
- Final **-r** remains before all voiced sounds except r-
- **l** never appears word-finally in Sanskrit
- Sandhi is EXTERNAL (cross-word) and systematic

**Relevance**: Sanskrit sandhi is driven by the FOLLOWING word's initial sound, not by the stem-final vowel. The Voynich pattern (stem-final vowel determines suffix) is fundamentally different from Sanskrit sandhi, though the cross-word component shows some parallels.

### Dravidian Languages (Tamil, Malayalam)

Dravidian languages have exceptionally rich n/l/r phonemic distinctions:

**Proto-Dravidian had:**
- Multiple nasals: dental /n/, alveolar /n-dot/, retroflex /N/
- Multiple laterals: alveolar /l/, retroflex /L/
- Multiple rhotics: tap /r/, trill-like sounds
- Five vowels: /a, e, i, o, u/

**Tamil sandhi rules involving n/l/r:**
- When /l/ or /L/ appear at stem end before a stop consonant, they change to nasal counterparts /n/ or /N/
- Between vowels, stops undergo lenition
- Heavy syllables become light before vowel-initial suffixes

**Key insight**: In Dravidian, consonant alternation (including n/l/r changes) IS conditioned by phonological environment, but the conditioning is by the FOLLOWING sound, not categorically by the PRECEDING vowel. The Voynich pattern (-i -> -n, -o -> -l, -a -> -r) has no direct parallel in Dravidian.

**However**: The Proto-Dravidian suffix extension system is intriguing. The "first possible extension is a vowel (V2), always a, i, or u" -- this mirrors the Voynich three-vowel set. And Dravidian is heavily suffixing.

### Turkish Vowel Harmony

Turkish provides a partial analogy:
- Suffixes change their vowel based on the stem's final vowel (vowel harmony)
- Two harmony patterns: twofold (e/a) and fourfold (i/u/i-dot/u-umlaut)
- Buffer consonants inserted between adjacent vowels

Turkish shows that vowel-conditioned suffix selection is typologically real, but in Turkish the VOWEL of the suffix changes, not the CONSONANT. The Voynich pattern (vowel determines consonant) is rarer.

### Korean Particle Allomorphy

Korean provides another partial analogy:
- Topic particle: -eun (after consonant) / -neun (after vowel)
- Subject particle: -i (after consonant) / -ga (after vowel)
- Object particle: -eul (after consonant) / -reul (after vowel)

Korean particles show consonant/vowel-conditioned allomorphy, but the conditioning is binary (consonant-final vs. vowel-final), not three-way based on vowel quality. The Voynich pattern requires the SPECIFIC vowel to determine the SPECIFIC consonant.

### Aymara Morphophonology

Aymara (three vowels: a, i, u) has:
- Dominant suffixes that delete preceding vowels
- Vowel deletion hierarchy: if /u/ is present, other vowels assimilate to it; between /i/ and /a/, /a/ assimilates
- Complex suffix-stem interaction

This is closer to the Voynich pattern in that specific vowel identity matters for morphophonological processes, but still does not show categorical vowel-to-consonant mapping.

### Bantu Noun Class Systems

Bantu languages show vowel-conditioned allomorphy in class prefixes:
- Class 5: allomorph /i-/ before consonants, /di-/ or /li-/ before vowels
- The specific consonant (d vs. l vs. r) in class 5 prefixes varies by language
- Agreement markers can be purely vocalic or CV

**Interesting parallel**: The variation between /d/, /l/, and /r/ in Bantu class 5 prefixes across languages suggests that n, l, r are prone to allomorphic variation cross-linguistically. But this is diachronic (across languages), not synchronic (within one language conditioned by vowel).

## 3. 15th-Century Italy: Contact Zone Languages

### Arabic (via Venetian Trade)

Venice was the major European center for trade with the Arabic-speaking world throughout the 15th century:
- Major ports of call: Istanbul, Damascus, Cairo, Aleppo, Alexandria
- Arabic medical and scientific texts were printed in Venice from the late 15th century
- Venetian merchants would have had regular exposure to Arabic

**Arabic fit with Voynich pattern:**
- Three vowels: a, i, u (close to Voynich a, i, o)
- Root-and-pattern morphology (consonantal root + vowel template)
- BUT: Arabic does not have the specific -i->n, -o->l, -a->r pattern
- Arabic's morphology is built around triconsonantal roots, not stem+suffix
- The Semitic hypothesis for Voynich (Hannig and others) focuses on Hebrew/Aramaic, not specifically this vowel pattern

### Jewish Communities in Northern Italy

- Ashkenazi Jews were the oldest Jewish community in Venice
- Sephardic and Italian Jewish groups also present
- Over 8,000 Hebrew parchment fragments discovered across Italy
- A unique 15th-century codex contained three medical manuscripts in **Judeo-Arabic** (Arabic written in Hebrew characters)
- Jews in Sicily and Apulia used Judeo-Arabic and Judeo-Latin

**Relevance**: Judeo-Arabic medical manuscripts in 15th-century Italy provide a direct precedent for a three-vowel Semitic language used for pharmaceutical/medical texts in the Voynich's time and place. The practice of writing one language in another's script (Judeo-Arabic = Arabic in Hebrew letters) also parallels the Voynich's potential use of an invented script for a known language.

### Ethiopian Christians at the Council of Florence (1441)

- Pope Eugenius IV contacted Ethiopian community in Jerusalem in 1440
- Ethiopian deacon Petros led a delegation to Florence in December 1441
- Coptic-Ethiopian embassy of twelve monks arrived from Cairo
- The Bull of Union was produced in both Latin and Arabic

**Relevance**: Ethiopian contact with Italy was real but limited. Ge'ez has 7 vowels (not 3), so a direct Ge'ez connection is unlikely. However, the contact demonstrates that non-European languages were present in 15th-century Italian intellectual circles.

### Armenian Merchants in Venice

- Armenians formed the oldest foreign merchant community in Venice
- Armenian was commonly heard in Venetian streets
- The first book printed in Armenian was printed in Venice (1512)
- Santa Croce degli Armeni church built in the 15th century
- Venetians and Armenians traded since Marco Polo's time

**Armenian fit:**
- Armenian has 6 vowels (a, e, i, o, u, schwa) -- too many
- 30+ consonants
- Plural suffix allomorphy: -er (after monosyllables), -ner (after polysyllables) -- phonologically conditioned
- No match to the -i->n, -o->l, -a->r pattern
- Armenian presence in Venice is well documented but the phonology doesn't match

### Berber (Tamazight) via North African Trade

- Three vowels: a, i, u -- matches the vowel count
- Root-and-pattern morphology (like Arabic)
- Nouns: masculine singular prefix a-, plural prefix i-
- Plurals end in -n

**Interesting**: Berber has three vowels AND uses -n as a plural marker, but the full -i->n, -o->l, -a->r mapping is not attested.

## 4. Constructed/Artificial Languages of the 15th Century

### Hildegard von Bingen's Lingua Ignota (12th century)

- Created by Benedictine Abbess Hildegard (1098-1179)
- Glossary of ~1,000 words arranged hierarchically (God, angels, humans, animals, plants)
- Used a 23-letter invented alphabet (litterae ignotae)
- Based on German and Latin with invented vocabulary
- Purpose: mystical/devotional, attributed to divine inspiration

**Relevance**: Proves that medieval Europeans DID create artificial languages with invented scripts. However, Lingua Ignota was a lexicon replacement (new words, same grammar) rather than a new phonological system.

### 15th-Century Cryptography

- The most common 15th-century cipher combined alphabetical letters, digits, and graphic signs
- Alchemists used extensive symbol systems for substances and processes
- Decknamen (code names) replaced common substance names
- The Naibbe cipher (2025 publication) demonstrates that a historically plausible 15th-century cipher using dice and cards can produce Voynich-like statistical properties from Latin/Italian plaintext

### Roger Bacon and Universal Language

- Roger Bacon (13th century) argued grammar is universal across languages
- The universal language movement proper began in the 17th century
- No documented 15th-century constructed language with designed phonology exists

### The Naibbe Cipher (2025 Research)

A significant recent finding: the Naibbe cipher is a verbose homophonic substitution cipher that:
- Can be performed entirely by hand with 15th-century materials (pen, paper, dice, cards)
- Encrypts Latin and Italian plaintext into Voynich-like ciphertext
- Reproduces many key statistical properties of the Voynich manuscript
- Uses dice throws to break text into units and card draws to select encryption tables

**Caveat**: The author states it is "almost certainly not the way the manuscript was constructed" but demonstrates that cipher-based explanations are viable. If the Voynich IS a cipher of Latin/Italian, the apparent three-vowel system and -i->n, -o->l, -a->r pattern could be artifacts of the encipherment process rather than properties of the underlying language.

## 5. The Specific Pattern: -i -> n, -o -> l, -a -> r

### Phonological Analysis

In articulatory phonetics:
- **/n/** = alveolar nasal (tongue tip touches alveolar ridge, air through nose)
- **/l/** = alveolar lateral (tongue tip touches alveolar ridge, air around sides)
- **/r/** = alveolar tap/trill (tongue tip briefly touches or vibrates against alveolar ridge)

All three are **alveolar sonorants** -- they share the same place of articulation and voicing. They differ only in manner:
- n = nasal
- l = lateral
- r = rhotic (tap/trill)

The vowels that condition them:
- **/i/** = high front vowel -> triggers **n** (nasal)
- **/o/** = mid back rounded vowel -> triggers **l** (lateral)
- **/a/** = low central vowel -> triggers **r** (rhotic)

### Is There a Phonetic Motivation?

Attempting to find articulatory motivation:
- **/i/** (high, front) -> **/n/** (nasal): High vowels are cross-linguistically associated with nasalization in some languages. The tongue position for /i/ (high front) is compatible with nasal closure.
- **/o/** (back, rounded) -> **/l/** (lateral): Less obvious. Lip rounding of /o/ could be associated with the "dark l" (velarized lateral) found in many languages.
- **/a/** (low, open) -> **/r/** (rhotic): The open mouth position of /a/ is somewhat compatible with the tongue-tip gesture for /r/.

**Verdict**: The phonetic motivation is weak at best. This pattern appears to be **morphological** rather than **phonetically natural**. It resembles an arbitrary but systematic morphophonological rule.

### Cross-Linguistic Search Results

**No natural language has been found where the stem-final vowel categorically determines a word-final consonant from the set {n, l, r}.**

The closest parallels are:
1. **Vowel harmony systems** (Turkish, Hungarian): Vowel determines vowel, not consonant
2. **Korean particle allomorphy**: Binary (C-final vs. V-final), not three-way
3. **Aymara vowel dominance**: Vowel identity matters but for vowel deletion, not consonant selection
4. **Bantu class markers**: Historically variable between d/l/r but not synchronically vowel-conditioned

### Implications

The absence of this pattern in any documented natural language suggests several possibilities:

1. **Artificial/constructed system**: The -i->n, -o->l, -a->r mapping was deliberately designed, possibly as a pharmaceutical notation or encoding system. The regularity and arbitrariness of the pattern is more consistent with a designed system than with natural language evolution.

2. **Cipher artifact**: If Voynich text is enciphered Latin/Italian (as the Naibbe cipher hypothesis suggests), the apparent vowel-consonant correlation could be a byproduct of the cipher algorithm rather than a linguistic feature.

3. **Undocumented natural language**: A language that has since become extinct or was never recorded by linguists. However, the typological rarity of this pattern makes this less likely.

4. **Mixed system**: A natural language base (possibly Arabic, Berber, or another three-vowel language) with an overlay of artificial morphological rules designed for a specific technical purpose (e.g., pharmaceutical classification).

## 6. Synthesis and Most Promising Candidates

### Ranked by Plausibility

**Tier 1: Highest plausibility**

1. **Deliberately constructed notation system** based in 15th-century Italian intellectual context
   - The -i->n, -o->l, -a->r pattern is too regular and typologically unique to be natural
   - Medical/pharmaceutical context of the manuscript supports specialized notation
   - Hildegard's Lingua Ignota proves the medieval precedent for invented languages
   - Could encode ingredient categories, preparation methods, or dosage via vowel classes

2. **Cipher of Latin/Italian** with the three-vowel pattern as a cipher artifact
   - The Naibbe cipher demonstrates viability
   - Would explain why no natural language matches
   - The manuscript's European provenance supports this

**Tier 2: Possible but less likely**

3. **Arabic-influenced system** from Venetian-Islamic contact zone
   - Three vowels match (a, i, u ~ a, i, o)
   - Extensive 15th-century Italian-Arabic contact documented
   - Judeo-Arabic medical manuscripts exist from this exact context
   - But Arabic morphology (root-and-pattern) does not produce the suffix pattern

4. **Berber-influenced system**
   - Three vowels (a, i, u) match
   - North African trade connections to Italy existed
   - Berber plural suffix -n is reminiscent
   - But full pattern unattested

**Tier 3: Unlikely but not impossible**

5. **Dravidian-family language**
   - Rich n/l/r phonemic distinctions
   - Complex sandhi systems
   - But five vowels (not three) and no direct 15th-century Italian contact
   - The n/l/r alternations in Dravidian are not vowel-conditioned in the Voynich way

6. **Nahuatl/Mesoamerican language**
   - Some researchers have proposed Aztec herbal connection
   - But dating conflicts with radiocarbon evidence (1404-1438)
   - And Nahuatl phonology does not match the three-vowel pattern

7. **Northwest Caucasian language** (Abkhaz, Adyghe)
   - Minimal vowel systems (2-3 vowels)
   - But geographically remote from 15th-century Italy
   - Consonant-rich systems don't match Voynich profile

## 7. The "Designed Pharmaceutical Notation" Hypothesis

The most parsimonious explanation for the -i->n, -o->l, -a->r pattern may be that it is an **artificial morphophonological rule** designed into a notation system:

### How it could work:

1. **Three vowel classes** represent three categories of pharmaceutical information:
   - Class A (-a words, suffix -r): e.g., ingredients/substances
   - Class I (-i words, suffix -n): e.g., preparations/processes
   - Class O (-o words, suffix -l): e.g., quantities/timing

2. **The suffix provides redundancy**: The suffix (n/l/r) echoes the vowel class, providing an error-checking mechanism. A reader could verify class membership by checking that the vowel-suffix pairing is correct.

3. **Cross-word sandhi provides sequencing information**: The influence of the following word's initial sound on suffix choice could encode relationships between consecutive elements (e.g., "this ingredient goes into that preparation").

4. **The system is not arbitrary but DESIGNED to be learnable**: Three vowels and three consonants, with a simple 1:1 mapping, is the kind of system a medieval pharmacist or natural philosopher might design for a specialized purpose.

### Supporting evidence:
- The manuscript's herbal and pharmaceutical content
- The 15th-century context of alchemical ciphers and pharmaceutical notation
- The Voynich manuscript's consistent internal logic despite resistance to decipherment
- The typological absence of this pattern in any natural language
- The regularity of the pattern (especially the near-100% -i -> -n mapping)

## 8. Open Questions for Further Investigation

1. **Does the Naibbe cipher reproduce the -i->n, -o->l, -a->r pattern?** If a known cipher algorithm generates this pattern from Latin/Italian, it would strongly support the cipher hypothesis.

2. **Are there unpublished pharmaceutical codices from 15th-century Italy** that use systematic abbreviation systems with vowel-consonant coding?

3. **Could "o" in the Voynich actually represent /u/?** If so, the system maps exactly to the canonical three-vowel system /a, i, u/ found in Arabic, Berber, and other languages.

4. **Is there a Judeo-Arabic pharmaceutical manuscript** from 15th-century Italy that shows any similar morphological pattern?

5. **Could the pattern arise from a specific encipherment of Arabic?** Arabic's three vowels (a, i, u) mapped through a substitution system could potentially generate Voynich-like text with vowel-conditioned suffixes if the cipher algorithm treats vowel-classes differently.
