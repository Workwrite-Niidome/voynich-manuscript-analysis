# Voynich Manuscript vs. 15th-Century Italian Nomenclators: A Structural Comparison

## 1. Structure of Surviving 15th-Century Italian Nomenclators

### 1.1 General Architecture

Italian diplomatic nomenclators of the 15th century were **hybrid systems** combining three elements:

1. **Monoalphabetic substitution cipher** -- each plaintext letter mapped to a symbol, number, or invented glyph
2. **Homophones** -- multiple cipher symbols for high-frequency letters (especially vowels a, e, i, o, u) to flatten frequency distributions
3. **Nomenclator list** -- a supplementary codebook of common words, names, and places, each assigned a unique symbol or short code

### 1.2 Organization of Entries

Nomenclator entries were organized in several ways across different Italian states:

- **By importance/topic**: Early nomenclators (1379-1420s) listed primarily **proper nouns** -- names of rulers, cities, the Pope, ambassadors. The 1401 Mantuan cipher's nomenclator contained entries like "Milan," "Florence," "Pope," "Modena."
- **By word class**: Later 15th-century nomenclators expanded to include **function words** (Come, Quando, Quanto, Non) alongside proper nouns.
- **By syllable**: The 1450 Mantuan cipher innovated by including **arbitrary shapes for syllable groups** (ab, ac, ad, af, ag, etc.), encoding sub-word units rather than complete words.
- **NOT strictly alphabetical**: Early nomenclators were organized by political/diplomatic relevance, not alphabetically. Alphabetical ordering became more common only in the 16th-17th centuries.

### 1.3 How Code Words Were Constructed

In 15th-century Italian nomenclators, the "code words" (cipher entries) took the following forms:

- **Arbitrary graphic symbols**: Invented shapes, crosses, dots, loops -- not pronounceable, not systematic
- **Two-letter code equivalents**: Gabriele de Lavinde's 1379 nomenclator used two-letter combinations for common words
- **Number shapes**: Milanese ciphers used "number shapes" typical of the "pre-Arabic digit phase" -- not standard numerals but number-like glyphs
- **Large graphic signs and Latin letters**: In the 15th century, graphic signs and Latin letters dominated nomenclatures; digits became more common only in the 16th-17th centuries

**Critical point**: The code entries were NOT pronounceable syllables. They were arbitrary symbols, short letter combinations, or graphic signs with no phonological structure.

### 1.4 Vocabulary Sizes

The growth of nomenclator vocabularies over time is well documented:

| Period | Typical Nomenclator Size | Examples |
|--------|-------------------------|----------|
| 1379 (Lavinde) | ~12-24 entries | "a dozen-or-so shapes enciphering entire words" |
| 1401 (Mantua) | ~80+ entries | "more than eighty special entries" |
| Early 15th c. | <50 entries (50% of keys) | Most keys had fewer than 50 nomenclature elements |
| Mid 15th c. | 21-100 entries (most common range) | Standard for Italian diplomatic use |
| Late 15th c. | 100+ entries becoming common | Growth trend toward larger vocabularies |
| 16th-17th c. | 1000+ entries | Spanish nomenclators under Philip II |
| 19th c. | Tens of thousands | Full codebooks replace nomenclators |

The Tranchedino ledger (Milan, post-1450) contained 287 complete keys plus 4 partial keys, with individual nomenclators typically containing a "dozen-or-so" to ~80 entries each. One documented key had 81 signs total: 36 for letters, 4 for double letters, 1 for nulls, 30 for syllables.

## 2. Luigi Pasini's 1872 Catalogue

### 2.1 The Work

Luigi Pasini (1835-1885) was an archivist at the Archivio di Stato di Venezia. His 1872 work, *"Delle scritture in cifra usate nella Repubblica di Venezia"* (On the Written Ciphers Used by the Republic of Venice), provides the first systematic catalogue of Venetian cipher keys.

### 2.2 Organization System

Pasini reorganized the cipher keys using a classification rule: **every cipher was identified by the sign(s) used to represent the letter A**. For example, a cipher might be classified as "A 16-36" based on its substitution for the vowel A.

### 2.3 Key Findings

- The oldest Venetian cipher example in the archive dates to **1411**
- The work documents the evolution from simple monoalphabetic ciphers to complex nomenclator systems
- Pasini decrypted many of the enciphered diplomatic messages he found

### 2.4 Modern Editions

- **2019**: Paolo Bonavoglia edited and extended Pasini's work
- **2021**: D.P.J.A. Scheers published an English translation
- Bonavoglia's 2021 paper "The ciphers of the Republic of Venice: an overview" (Cryptologia, Vol. 46, No. 4) provides the most comprehensive modern analysis based on a two-year research project at the State Archives of Venice

## 3. Specific Surviving Codebooks

### 3.1 Mantua 1401

- **Type**: First known homophonic substitution cipher
- **Innovation**: Mixed alphabet letters with new letters AND substituted additional symbols for each vowel
- **Multiple homophones**: Allocated multiple shapes to each of the five vowels
- **Nomenclator**: "More than eighty special entries" for common words and names
- **Purpose**: Correspondence with the King of Hungary

### 3.2 Florence 1424

- **Known as**: Cifra di Galiotto Fibindacci da Ricasoli (1424)
- **Feature**: Used "4o" for the letter Q
- **Structure**: Similar hybrid cipher+nomenclator format documented by Meister

### 3.3 Milan -- Tranchedino Ledger (post-1450)

- **Creator**: Francesco Tranchedino under Cicco Simonetta, after Francesco Sforza's takeover of Milan in 1450
- **Date**: The surviving cryptographical collection originates from 1475
- **Scale**: 6 deciphered ciphers + 287 complete keys + 4 partial keys
- **Contents**: Substitution alphabets, digraphs, trigraphs, nomenclator entries (predominantly proper nouns with coded equivalents), and "dummies" (meaningless symbols to thwart decipherment)
- **Character set**: "Well over 50" symbols per cipher key, including multiple symbols for frequent plaintext characters, nulls, doublets, and nomenclator words
- **Nomenclator entries**: Typically place/person names -- "Milan, Florence, Pope, Modena, etc."

### 3.4 Agostino Amadi's Venetian Cipher Manuscript (pre-1588)

- **Scale**: 700 pages divided into 10 volumes
- **Contents**: More than 80 different ciphering methods with diagrams, tables, symbolic images
- **Structure**: Theoretical part (cipher introductions, methods, examples) + practical part (decipherment exercises for novice cipher clerks)
- **Historical significance**: Kept in the closed archives of the Venetian Council of Ten; bought from Amadi's widow in 1588
- **Modern publication**: Translated and published by D.P.J.A. Scheers (3 volumes)

### 3.5 Cicco Simonetta's Treatise on Decipherment

- **Role**: Head of the Sforza-era Milanese cipher department
- **Scope**: Almost all ciphertexts that arrived were in Latin or Tuscan
- **Method**: Developed systematic frequency-analysis approaches to breaking ciphers

## 4. Pharmaceutical/Alchemical Code Systems

### 4.1 Alchemical Decknamen (Code Names)

A parallel tradition of encoding existed outside diplomacy: the alchemical **Decknamen** system. These were code words used by alchemists and herbalists to conceal the identities of ingredients.

**Key characteristics**:
- **Format**: Animal or mythological names substituted for plant names
- **Organization**: Alphabetically organized by code name (A-W in surviving lists)
- **Size**: Approximately **160+ individual code entries** documented from sources including the Greek Magical Papyri (200 BC-500 AD), Galen, and Dioscorides
- **Structure**: Simple one-to-one substitution (code name -> plant), no agglutinative structure

**Examples**:
| Code Name (Decknamen) | Actual Plant |
|----------------------|-------------|
| Adder's Tongue | Dogstooth Violet or Plantain |
| Bat's Wing | Holly Leaf |
| Bull's Blood / Seed of Horus | Horehound |
| Cat | Catnip |
| Devil's Dung | Asafoetida |
| Dragon's Blood | Resin of Draco palm |
| Dragon's Scales | Bistort Leaves |
| Dog's Mouth | Snapdragon |
| Eagle | Wild Garlic or Fenugreek |
| Frog's Foot | Bulbous Buttercup |
| Lion's Tooth | Dandelion |
| Wolf's Milk | Euphorbia |

### 4.2 Apothecary Cipher Usage

- No documented cases of pharmacists using **formal cipher systems** (monoalphabetic substitution, nomenclators) for recipes
- Instead, apothecaries relied on **Latin/Greek terminology** as a natural barrier to lay understanding
- **Alchemists** did use formal ciphers: a 16th-century coded text used a Bellaso/Della Porta polyalphabetic cipher to encrypt a recipe for the "Philosopher's Stone"
- The Decknamen tradition was **inherited** rather than invented -- medieval and Renaissance grimoires passed down ancient code names from Greco-Roman sources

### 4.3 Relevance to the Voynich

The Decknamen system is relevant because:
- It was a **domain-specific** code for botanical/pharmaceutical content
- It had a vocabulary of ~160 entries -- comparable to the Voynich's ~100-200 distinct roots
- It was used in exactly the kind of herbal/pharmaceutical context depicted in the Voynich manuscript
- However, it used **natural-language words** (animal names) as codes, not invented syllables

## 5. Critical Structural Comparison: Nomenclators vs. Voynich Word System

### 5.1 Were Code Words Pronounceable?

| Feature | Diplomatic Nomenclators | Voynich Words |
|---------|------------------------|---------------|
| Pronounceability | **NO** -- arbitrary symbols, graphic signs, number-shapes | **YES** -- words follow consistent phonotactic rules |
| Phonological structure | None -- symbols have no sound value | Clear consonant-vowel patterns with positional constraints |
| Reading aloud | Impossible -- symbols are visual-only | Appears designed for oral reading/pronunciation |

### 5.2 Were They Arbitrary Letter Combinations?

| Feature | Diplomatic Nomenclators | Voynich Words |
|---------|------------------------|---------------|
| Code format | Arbitrary graphic symbols, two-letter codes, number-shapes | Systematic glyph combinations following strict rules |
| Internal structure | No internal structure -- each code is an atomic unit | Rich internal structure: prefix + core + suffix |
| Combinatorial logic | Code entries chosen arbitrarily | Words built from a finite set of elements via combinatorial rules |

### 5.3 Did They Use Agglutinative Structure?

| Feature | Diplomatic Nomenclators | Voynich Words |
|---------|------------------------|---------------|
| Morphological structure | **NONE** -- each code word is a monolithic symbol | **YES** -- systematic prefix + midfix + suffix (Stolfi's model) |
| Compositionality | Codes cannot be decomposed into meaningful parts | Words decompose into ~5 positional slots (crust-mantle-core-mantle-crust) |
| Generative capacity | Fixed list -- no way to generate new codes | Combinatorial system generates thousands of words from ~100-200 elements |
| Parallel in cipher history | No known nomenclator uses agglutinative code construction | **Unprecedented** in any known 15th-century cipher system |

### 5.4 Vocabulary Size Comparison

| System | Vocabulary Size |
|--------|----------------|
| Lavinde 1379 nomenclator | ~12-24 entries |
| Mantua 1401 nomenclator | ~80 entries |
| Typical 15th c. nomenclator | 21-100 entries (most common) |
| Alchemical Decknamen | ~160 entries |
| Voynich manuscript distinct "words" | ~8,000+ word types |
| Voynich manuscript distinct "roots" | ~100-200 estimated |
| 16th c. Spanish nomenclators | 1,000+ entries |

The Voynich's **root inventory** (~100-200) matches the range of a mid-to-large 15th-century nomenclator or the alchemical Decknamen system. But the **surface vocabulary** (8,000+ word types) far exceeds any 15th-century nomenclator -- this is possible only because of the agglutinative/combinatorial word-formation system.

### 5.5 Homophones

| Feature | Diplomatic Nomenclators | Voynich Words |
|---------|------------------------|---------------|
| Homophones present? | **YES** -- multiple symbols for high-frequency letters, especially vowels | **Possibly** -- some words appear interchangeable in similar contexts |
| Purpose | Flatten frequency distributions to resist cryptanalysis | Unknown -- possibly similar anti-frequency purpose |
| Scale | 2-5 homophones per vowel typical | Unclear -- homophone identification is debated |

## 6. The Voynich's Unique Features vs. Known Nomenclators

### 6.1 Features That Match Nomenclators

| Voynich Feature | Nomenclator Parallel | Assessment |
|-----------------|---------------------|------------|
| ~100-200 distinct roots | Mid-large nomenclator range (80-200 entries) | **COMPATIBLE** |
| Section-specific vocabulary | Nomenclators organized by topic/importance | **PARTIALLY COMPATIBLE** |
| First word on herbal pages may be plant name | Nomenclator entries for proper nouns | **COMPATIBLE** |
| Two "languages" (Currier A and B) | Different cipher keys for different correspondents | **WEAKLY COMPATIBLE** |
| Labels near illustrations | Nomenclator entries labeling persons/places | **COMPATIBLE** |

### 6.2 Features That DO NOT Match Any Known Nomenclator

| Voynich Feature | Nomenclator Behavior | Assessment |
|-----------------|---------------------|------------|
| **Agglutinative word structure** (prefix+core+suffix) | Codes are atomic symbols with no internal structure | **NO MATCH -- unprecedented** |
| **Slot grammar** (12 positional slots with constrained glyph choices) | No positional constraints within code words | **NO MATCH** |
| **Sandhi rules** (context-dependent glyph changes at word boundaries) | No boundary-sensitive phenomena in any nomenclator | **NO MATCH -- unique to Voynich** |
| **Pronounceable words** following phonotactic rules | Codes are arbitrary unpronounceable symbols | **NO MATCH** |
| **Generative word formation** (small element set produces large vocabulary) | Fixed lists -- no generative mechanism | **NO MATCH** |
| **Very low entropy** (h2 ~2.0 vs natural language 3-4) | Nomenclator ciphertext has normal or higher entropy | **NO MATCH** |
| **Zipfian word frequency distribution** | Nomenclator ciphertext does not produce Zipfian distributions | **PARTIALLY ADDRESSED by Naibbe cipher** |

### 6.3 The Naibbe Cipher: Bridging the Gap?

The **Naibbe cipher** (Greshko, 2025) represents the most serious attempt to explain the Voynich as a cipher system:

- **Mechanism**: Verbose homophonic substitution mapping individual plaintext letters onto multiple Voynichese glyph strings
- **Word structure**: Output follows an "expanded version of the slot grammar" observed in the manuscript
- **Historical plausibility**: Can be performed by hand with 15th-century materials (playing cards/tarocchi)
- **Statistical success**: Reproduces Zipfian distribution, many key statistical properties of Voynich A
- **Limitation**: "Incomplete replication of Voynich B's properties" -- does not fully explain the two-language phenomenon
- **Key difference from nomenclators**: The Naibbe cipher is NOT a nomenclator. It maps individual **letters** to glyph strings, not words to codes. It is a verbose homophonic substitution cipher.

## 7. Synthesis: What Can We Conclude?

### 7.1 The Voynich Is NOT a Standard Nomenclator

The evidence is clear: the Voynich manuscript's word structure is fundamentally incompatible with any known 15th-century nomenclator. Nomenclators use atomic, arbitrary symbols for words. The Voynich uses a generative, agglutinative system with positional constraints, sandhi rules, and phonotactic structure. No surviving Italian nomenclator from any period exhibits these features.

### 7.2 The Root Inventory Matches a Domain-Specific Codebook

The Voynich's ~100-200 distinct roots are consistent with:
- A mid-to-large 15th-century nomenclator (80-200 entries)
- The alchemical Decknamen tradition (~160 code names for plants)
- A specialized pharmaceutical or botanical vocabulary

This suggests the **semantic scope** of the Voynich is comparable to a domain-specific code, even if the **encoding mechanism** is fundamentally different.

### 7.3 Three Possibilities

1. **Verbose homophonic cipher** (Naibbe-type): Individual letters of a Latin/Italian plaintext are mapped to glyph strings via a structured substitution table. This explains the slot grammar and Zipfian distribution but struggles with Voynich B and sandhi rules.

2. **Constructed notation system**: The Voynich script is a purpose-built notation (like shorthand or a constructed language) for recording domain-specific knowledge (herbal/pharmaceutical). The agglutinative structure encodes meaning compositionally -- roots for ingredients, affixes for preparation methods, quantities, or properties.

3. **Natural language in an unknown/exotic script**: The word structure reflects a real agglutinative language transcribed in a unique alphabet. The section-specific vocabulary reflects topical content rather than a codebook structure.

### 7.4 What Nomenclator Research Rules Out

The comparison with actual surviving 15th-century Italian nomenclators conclusively eliminates the hypothesis that the Voynich manuscript is a straightforward nomenclator cipher of the type used in Italian diplomatic correspondence. The structural differences are too fundamental: no nomenclator produces pronounceable, agglutinative words with positional glyph constraints and sandhi rules.

However, the **scale** of the Voynich's root vocabulary and its **topical organization** are entirely consistent with the world of 15th-century Italian code systems -- suggesting the author was likely aware of or influenced by the nomenclator tradition, even if the actual system employed was something different and more sophisticated.
