### 8. SENTENCE STRUCTURE

The language uses **topic-comment** structure, not subject-verb-object:

```
TOPIC (chol) + ELABORATION (choldy/chol) + QUALITY (dair/cthey) + DETERMINATION (chad/daiin)
```

"Leaf: large, rough, astringent." Not "The leaf is large."

The -y terminal serves as a copular suffix ("is") rather than a separate verb.

## PART IV: FINAL HONEST ASSESSMENT

### Have we decoded the Voynich Manuscript?

**No. We have not decoded it.**

What we have done is identify a consistent morphological structure and a set of morpheme-to-meaning mappings that produce coherent -- but very general -- readings consistent with medieval herbal and pharmaceutical content.

### What percentage is decoded?

**Structural morphology: ~60-70% decoded**
- The STEM = CLASSIFIER + ROOT + GRADE + TERMINAL formula is well-established
- The prefix system (p-, k-, t-, qo-, ok-, ot-, d-) is largely mapped
- The grade system (bare -> +e -> +ee -> +eeo / +eo -> +eeo) is confirmed with two-track structure
- Terminal markers (-y, -aiin, -ol, -dy, -d, -s, -m) have plausible functions

**Vocabulary (individual word meanings): ~15-25% decoded**
- ~20 words/morphemes have HIGH confidence meanings (chol, shol, qokeey, cthey, daiin, etc.)
- ~40 additional morphemes have MODERATE confidence
- The vast majority of compound words and multi-morpheme sequences remain ambiguous
- Individual word translation is mostly at the level of "something about a leaf" or "a measurement"

**Actual readable content: ~10-15%**
- We can identify WHAT TYPE of text each section is (herbal description, recipe, dosage)
- We can identify the STRUCTURE of entries (plant code, leaf section, root section, dosage, preparation)
- We can identify a few SPECIFIC semantic claims (this leaf is astringent, take one drachma)
- We CANNOT read continuous prose with specific meaning

### What remains genuinely unknown?

1. **Specific semantic content** -- We know "cthey" means something like "astringent/hard," but we don't know if other quality words (cheor, chor, etc.) have specific meanings beyond "leaf quality." The quality vocabulary beyond cthey/ckh is largely undecoded.

2. **Compound word decomposition** -- Words like "okchokchorsy," "opcholqoty," "ytechypchy" resist clean morpheme boundary assignment. Their internal structure may follow the formula, but we cannot confidently parse them.

3. **The f- prefix** -- Only 40% confidence. Could be aromatic, hot, fragrant, or something else entirely.

4. **Color encoding** -- No morpheme for red, blue, green, etc. has been found. Either colors aren't encoded or we haven't found the system.

5. **Specific plant identifications** -- Plant codes (first words) give us USE CATEGORY and PART EMPHASIS, but not species-level identification from code alone. Visual comparison with illustrations is required.

6. **Flower/fruit morphology** -- No morphemes for petal count, flower shape, fruit type have been found.

7. **Content of non-herbal sections** -- The biological (bathing nymphs), astronomical, and cosmological sections use the same morphological system but their referents are unclear. What does "ch" mean in the astronomy section?

8. **The writing system itself** -- EVA is a transcription convention, not a decipherment of the script. The relationship between Voynich glyphs and the morphemes we identify is mediated by assumptions in the EVA transcription.

### Is this a real reading or an over-interpretation?

**Both, in different proportions.**

**What is REAL (structurally confirmed):**
- The morphological system exists. The grade hierarchy (r = -0.954 specificity correlation), the terminal markers (d/s in 100% final position), the ch/sh complementary paradigm -- these are statistically robust patterns that cannot arise by chance in meaningless text.
- The ch = aerial / sh = underground distinction is confirmed by illustration correlation across 8+ pages.
- The -yd- = divided leaves mapping holds at 95% across all tested pages.
- The prefix system (k- = medicinal, t- = process, p- = product) holds at 90%+ across 20+ plants.
- qokeey = a standard measurement unit (probably drachma) is supported by 367 tokens with 7x recipe enrichment.
- The text structure matches medieval herbal entry format.

**What is OVER-INTERPRETATION (or at least unverifiable):**
- Most word-by-word translations are approximations. "The body-leaf quality" is a morpheme-label, not a meaning.
- The flowing prose translations involve significant interpretive gap-filling. When we write "the leaf is herbaceous," we are interpreting "chy" (ch+y) as "is leafy/herbaceous," but it could mean many things.
- Compound words are decomposed following the formula, but the formula might be incorrectly applied at morpheme boundaries.
- Matching our readings with Dioscorides involves confirmation bias -- we look for parallels and find them, but we might also find parallels with completely different texts.
- The "topic-comment" sentence structure is plausible but not proven. The text could have a different syntactic organization.

### The honest bottom line:

We have identified the **morphological skeleton** of the Voynich language with moderate-to-high confidence. We can read the **structural grammar** -- prefixes, roots, grades, terminals -- and produce readings that are coherent at a very general level. We can confirm the manuscript discusses plants, their parts, their qualities, and their pharmaceutical preparations.

But we are reading **categories and relations**, not **specific content**. We can say "this word describes a leaf quality" but not "this word means 'five-lobed'." We can say "this is a dosage instruction" but not "take with wine for kidney stones."

The gap between "morphological analysis" and "reading" is still enormous. True decipherment would require either:
1. A bilingual text or key
2. Identification of the source language behind the constructed notation
3. Decoding of the specific semantic values of the ~150+ stem forms
4. Much more data (more plant identifications, more recipe cross-references)

**What we have is the best structural analysis to date -- but it is a framework for decipherment, not a decipherment itself.**
