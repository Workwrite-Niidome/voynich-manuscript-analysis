# Marci's Annotations on Folio 1r: Lazarus Project Multispectral Imaging Analysis

## 1. Background: The Lazarus Project Imaging

In 2014, The Lazarus Project team (Michael Phelps, Gregory Heyworth, Chet Van Duzer, Ken Boydston, Roger Easton) was granted permission to take multispectral images of **ten select pages** of the Voynich Manuscript while working on another imaging project at Yale's Beinecke Library. The results were analyzed and published in September 2024 by **Lisa Fagin Davis**, executive director of the Medieval Academy of America.

Pages imaged: f1r, f17r, f26r, f70v1, f71r, f93r, f102v1, f116v, and others.

---

## 2. What Multispectral Imaging Revealed on f1r

The right margin of f1r contains a heavily stained ~1-inch band that was previously almost illegible. Under UV and multispectral processing, **three parallel columns of faded lettering** became visible:

### Column 1: Roman Alphabet (a-z)
- Lowercase italic hand, running a through z
- Letters a-e were partially visible even in standard light
- Full alphabet became legible under UV

### Column 2: Voynich Characters
- "Very spotty fragments" of Voynichese glyphs paired with Roman letters
- Specifically identified (using EVA notation):
  - **EVA `<8>` (= `d` in some systems) next to `a`**
  - **EVA `<R>` next to `c`**
  - **EVA `<6>` next to `y`**
  - **A gallows character somewhere near the `q`, `r`, `s` range**
- Most Voynich characters in the column appear elsewhere in the manuscript
- **The last two characters** (adjacent to y and z) appear **nowhere else** in the known text -- suggesting they may have existed on now-missing leaves

### Column 3: Roman Alphabet (offset by one)
- Another set of italic minuscules
- Shifted up by one position from Column 1 (e.g., `r` appears next to `s`)
- Slightly more displaced from the other two columns

### Interpretation
This structure suggests a **substitution cipher table**: Roman letter -> Voynich glyph -> shifted Roman letter. However, Davis emphasizes: "these alphabets will likely *not* help us actually decipher the manuscript" because the Voynich text is almost certainly not a simple substitution cipher, and applying these substitutions produces nonsense.

---

## 3. Attribution to Johannes Marcus Marci

Davis performed paleographic analysis comparing the revealed handwriting to known correspondence of individuals connected to the manuscript. The handwriting matched **Johannes Marcus Marci (1595-1667)**, a Prague physician and rector of Charles University.

### Matching Characteristics:
- Loop-less b, d, f, h, p, q, s, and y
- Open-bowl g formation
- Distinctive m with shrinking/taller-first-stroke pattern
- Characteristic z shape

### Timeline:
- Marci inherited the manuscript from Georg Baresch (~1662)
- Sent it to Athanasius Kircher in Rome (1665/1666)
- The annotations date to this 3-year window

---

## 4. Marci's Partial Mapping: What We Know

Based on the fragmentary evidence recovered:

| Roman Letter | Voynich Glyph (EVA) | Shifted Roman |
|---|---|---|
| a | `<8>` (EVA d?) | b? |
| b | ? | c? |
| c | `<R>` | d? |
| ... | ... | ... |
| q-r-s range | gallows char | shifted |
| ... | ... | ... |
| y | `<6>` | z? |
| z | unknown char* | ? |

*The characters next to y and z do not appear anywhere else in the manuscript.

### Does This Match Any Known Cipher System?
- The two Roman columns offset by one suggest a **Caesar cipher (shift +1)**, the simplest substitution
- The Voynich column in between suggests Marci was trying to build a **trilateral table**: plaintext -> cipher -> alternative plaintext
- This is consistent with 17th-century cryptographic methods (Marci would have been familiar with Kircher's and others' work)
- **It does not match any known working decipherment of the manuscript**

### Does It Match Our Syllable Hypothesis?
- Our hypothesis treats Voynich glyphs as syllabic units, not individual letter substitutions
- Marci's approach was strictly alphabetic (1:1 letter-to-glyph mapping)
- The fact that his mapping produces nonsense is **consistent with** the syllabic hypothesis -- if glyphs represent syllables, then letter-level substitution would indeed yield gibberish
- However, individual glyph identifications (e.g., EVA `<8>` = `a`) could potentially represent the *initial consonant or vowel* of a syllable

## 5. Annotations Visible in Standard Scan

Looking at the standard Beinecke scan (page_003.png = f1r):
- The right margin shows a **darkened, stained vertical band** running along the edge
- Letters a through approximately e can be faintly discerned in high-resolution standard scans
- The bulk of the three-column structure is **invisible without multispectral processing**
- The red/brown decorative initial characters ("weirdo doodles", EVA &252 and &253) at paragraph openings are clearly visible
- The erased Jacobus de Tepenecz signature at the top is barely discernible as faint cursive

## 7. Annotations on Other Folios

The multispectral imaging revealed findings on multiple pages:

### f17r
- A line of writing in Latin script in the top margin
- **Same hand as f116v** with 99% certainty (identical character formations)
- By the same person who wrote the "michitonese" text

### f26r
- Mirror-image offset from f25v
- Show-through from f26v (prominent with green pigment)
- Mysterious semi-circular mark in upper margin (purpose unknown)

### f70v1
- Pale blue text visible in MSI proved to be **show-through from f70r1**, not hidden writing
- Critical lesson in distinguishing show-through from genuine hidden text

### f71r
- Only zodiac page with significant color
- Colors respond differently to MSI (turquoise glows yellow; green appears light blue)
- Potential for X-ray fluorescence pigment analysis

### f93r
- Pigment stain matching flower color, with text written **over** it
- Confirms illustrations were completed **before** text was added

### f102v1
- Clarified drawing beneath rust-colored stain
- Revealed obscured text on blue vessel portion
- Recovered faded text on heavily-damaged fold

## 8. The "Michiton Oladabas" Inscription (f116v)

### Historical Readings
The final page contains 3-4 lines of mysterious marginalia in distorted Latin script. Key transcription attempts:

| Scholar | Era | Reading |
|---|---|---|
| Newbold | 1920s | "michiton oladabas multos te tccr cerc portas" |
| Brumbaugh | 1970s | "MICHI CON OLADA BA" |
| Koen G. (2023) | Modern | "poxleber pinen p?tpfer + anchiton oladabad + ???l.tos + te + cere + portad + N + six + marix + morix + vix + abia + maria + palden pbren so nim gasmich" |

### Identified Words (Modern Analysis)
- **poxleber** = Bocksleber = goat's liver (German)
- **maria** = Mary (religious reference)
- **so nim** = "so take" (German)
- **gasmich** = Geissmilch = goat's milk (German)
- **vix** = Wichs = wax (German)

The text appears to be a **recipe or charm** mixing German dialect words with Latin fragments, possibly from the Eastern Bavarian/Vienna region.

### Multispectral Imaging Results for f116v
- **Disappointing results** overall -- insufficient contrast in the JPEGs for the top line ("pox leber" line)
- Characters previously thought to begin with [p] were revealed to begin with **[u] or [v]**
- Apparent letter descenders proved to be **stains, not ink**
- Show-through from f116r created interpretive challenges
- Multiple layers of ink detected -- the text has been **overwritten and emended** by different hands
- The 'l' of "oladabas" appears written by a 15th-century quill, but the 'n' of "michiton" shows 16th-century metal nib characteristics

### Nick Pelling's "Nichil Obstat" Theory
Pelling proposed that "michiton oladabas" might originally have read **"nichil obstat"** (Latin: "nothing stands in the way" -- a Catholic imprimatur phrase) before fading and being emended by a later owner. The word "ahia maria" could represent "Saint Mary" in Greek pronunciation.

### Key Insight
f116v is **not a single-layer text**. It contains at least two codicological layers: an original 15th-century inscription and 16th-century emendations/corrections. Understanding the page requires **separating these layers**, which multispectral imaging alone could not fully achieve. Raman imaging or surface depth mapping would be needed.

---

## 9. Broader Implications

### For Decipherment
- Marci's substitution cipher attempt on f1r **failed** -- further evidence against simple substitution
- The f116v marginalia remains unresolved even with modern imaging
- Neither finding provides a "Rosetta Stone" for the manuscript

### For Authenticity
- The faded annotations would be impractical for a modern forger to create intentionally
- The imaging results support the manuscript's authenticity as a genuine medieval document
- Pigment analysis (f93r) confirms illustrations preceded text -- consistent with genuine production

### For Future Research
- Davis emphasizes these results are "a clear proof-of-concept" that more imaging of more pages would yield additional hidden evidence
- Full 16-bit scans (not compressed JPEGs) are needed for definitive f116v analysis
- X-ray fluorescence could identify pigment composition
- Surface depth mapping could separate ink layers on f116v
