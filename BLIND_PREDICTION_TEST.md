## PART IV: STATISTICAL ANALYSIS

### Chance Baseline Estimation

To determine if our accuracy exceeds chance, we need base rates for each visual feature in Voynich herbal illustrations:

**Base rates (estimated from general Voynich herbal characteristics):**

Most Voynich herbal illustrations show a complete plant with roots, stem, leaves, and often flowers. Typical proportions:

1. **"Leaves prominent"**: ~70% of herbal pages show leaves as a major visual element (they are plants, after all). This means predicting "leaves prominent" whenever ch is present is easy to get right.

2. **"Roots prominent"**: ~50% of herbal pages show notable root systems (the Voynich artist often depicts roots in red/brown).

3. **"No prominent roots"**: ~50% (inverse of above).

4. **"No divided leaves"**: ~70% of plants have simple/undivided leaves (divided leaves are a minority morphology). Predicting "not divided" is the safe bet.

5. **"No prominent flowers"**: ~40% of herbal pages lack prominent flowers. Many Voynich illustrations DO show flowers. So predicting "no flowers" is actually going against the base rate.

6. **"Divided leaves present"**: ~30% of plants show divided/lobed leaves.

### Critical Assessment

**What works:**
- The **ch -> "leaves prominent"** prediction achieved 100% accuracy. However, with a ~70% base rate, this is not dramatic. With only 4 data points, we cannot distinguish 100% from 70% with statistical confidence (p = 0.70^4 = 0.24 for getting all right by chance alone, which is NOT significant).

- The **"no divided leaves" (yd absent)** prediction achieved 80%, vs. ~70% base rate. With 2 misses in 10 (f28r and f36r -- both of which actually HAVE divided/lobed leaves and LACK the yd morpheme), the system CORRECTLY identified 8 of 10 but the 2 false negatives are problematic: f28r shows a deeply lobed leaf and f36r shows deeply lobed leaves, yet neither word contains -yd-. The system SHOULD have predicted "divided leaves" for these but could not because the morpheme was absent.

**What fails:**
- The **sh -> "roots prominent"** prediction failed catastrophically: 0.5/2 = 25%. f26r (psheoky, sh present) does NOT show prominent roots -- leaves dominate. f33r (tshdar, sh present) shows moderate roots but flowers dominate.

- The **sh absent -> "no prominent roots"** prediction scored only 43.8%. Four of eight illustrations WITHOUT sh in the first word (f22r, f28r, f31r, f34r) actually show prominent roots. This means the sh morpheme does NOT reliably predict root prominence: pages WITH sh don't show prominent roots, and pages WITHOUT sh often DO show prominent roots.

- The **f38r "no part emphasis"** prediction was wrong: the illustration is overwhelmingly leaf-dominated, but the word `tolor` contains neither ch nor sh. This suggests the morpheme system cannot explain ALL leaf/root emphasis patterns.

### Overall Verdict

**Overall accuracy: 67.6% (23 + 2 partial out of 37 predictions)**

**Adjusted for chance: Marginal at best.**

The system performs roughly at or slightly above chance for most prediction types. The only strong result is "ch predicts leaves prominent" (4/4), but this has a high base rate (~70%) and too few data points for significance.

The sh morpheme FAILS to predict root prominence. This is the most damaging finding: the cornerstone claim that sh = "underground/root" does not survive blind testing on new folios.

### Honest Conclusion

**The descriptive naming system as formulated does NOT pass this blind prediction test.** While some predictions (ch -> leaves, yd absent -> undivided) perform at or slightly above chance, the key morpheme-to-feature mappings (sh -> roots) fail. The overall accuracy of ~68% is not significantly above what a naive predictor would achieve by exploiting base rates (estimated ~60-65%).

The system's apparent success in prior analyses was likely driven by:
1. Post-hoc fitting (seeing the illustration first, then finding morphemes that match)
2. High base rates for common predictions (most plants have prominent leaves)
3. Cherry-picking anchor plants where the system happened to work
4. Not testing negative predictions (absence of morphemes)

**This does not disprove that the Voynich text has meaningful morphological structure** (the shuffling test in the Red Team report demonstrated real non-random page-level clustering). But the SPECIFIC morpheme-to-visual-feature mappings claimed by the descriptive naming hypothesis are not validated by this blind test.
