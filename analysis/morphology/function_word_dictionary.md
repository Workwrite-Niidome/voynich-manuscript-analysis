## 2. Distributional Analysis of Major Function Words

### daiin (freq=674) -- Proposed: "of" (genitive marker)

**Position in line**: avg=0.48 (evenly distributed), start=194, mid=314, end=166
- Appears everywhere in lines -- consistent with a high-frequency preposition

**Top preceding words** (what comes before "daiin"):
1. chol (26) -- a content word (plant part?)
2. ol (10) -- another function word
3. chey (9), qokeey (9) -- content words
4. daiin (8) -- can self-chain: "of... of..."
5. qoky (8), chy (7), chor (7)

**Top following words** (what comes after "daiin"):
1. shey (13), chey (13) -- content words
2. cthy (8), chy (8) -- content words
3. daiin (8) -- self-chaining
4. cthor (7), or (7), ol (7), al (7)

**Deep analysis**:
- **Between two words (X daiin Y)**: 424 (63%) -- STRONG evidence for genitive/possessive function
- **Line-initial (daiin Y)**: 146 (22%)
- **Line-final (X daiin)**: 104 (15%)

**Following words by semantic type**: cho/sho/cth-initial words follow 16% of the time. This is moderate, not overwhelming, suggesting "daiin" connects many types of words, not just plant terminology.

**Section distribution**: Remarkably even across all sections (herbal: 426, astro: 21, cosmo: 22, pharma: 69, recipes: 27, stars: 109). This universality is exactly what we'd expect from a function word like "of."

**Verdict**: HIGH CONFIDENCE that daiin = "of" / genitive marker. It overwhelmingly appears between two content words (X daiin Y pattern in 63% of cases). The most common pattern is `chol daiin` ("... of the leaf/plant?").

---

### aiin (freq=613) -- Proposed: "and" or copula/connector

**Position in line**: avg=0.56, start=93, mid=358, end=162
- Slightly mid-to-end biased

**Top preceding words**:
1. **or (51)** -- extremely strong association!
2. **s (50)** -- extremely strong association!
3. **r (29), ar (29)** -- strong
4. **ol (23)** -- strong
5. o (12), d (7), ches (7), cheo (7), sar (7)

**Top following words**:
1. chey (13), ol (13), al (10)
2. okal (8), okaiin (8), otar (8)
3. otaiin (7), or (7), y (7), shey (7)

**Critical observation**: Bare "aiin" is overwhelmingly preceded by single-character or two-character words (or, s, r, ar, ol). This strongly suggests that what we read as "or aiin" or "s aiin" may actually be morphological units where the prefix has been separated by a word boundary in the transcription, OR that "aiin" is a grammatical connector that follows short function words.

**Section distribution**: Extremely concentrated in Stars section (223/613 = 36%), also strong in herbal3 (105), pharma (59), recipes (60). Very rare in herbal1 (6) and herbal2 (14). This section bias suggests it may serve a different function in different sections, or that certain text types use it more.

**Verdict**: MEDIUM CONFIDENCE. Two possibilities:
1. "aiin" = "and" / conjunction (connecting elements in lists)
2. "aiin" = bare case marker, with the "prefix" being the preceding word stem (i.e., "or aiin" = a single morphological unit)

The extremely strong `or + aiin` and `s + aiin` bigrams favor interpretation #2 -- that the word boundary is artificial and these are really morphological units.

### otaiin (freq=147) and okaiin (freq=198) -- Compound prefixed forms

These high-frequency forms contain the prefix sequences **ot-** and **ok-** which are themselves among the most productive in the manuscript.

**otaiin**: avg position=0.58, slightly end-biased. Concentrated in stars (f103-f116).
- Preceded by: aiin (7), okaiin (6) -- often chains with other -aiin words
- Followed by: otal (6), otar (5), ol (4), al (4)

**okaiin**: avg position=0.50. Also concentrated in stars.
- Preceded by: aiin (8), chey (6), daiin (5)
- Followed by: otaiin (6), shedy (6)

These appear to be prefixed (agglutinated) forms. If ot- and ok- are content morphemes, then ot+aiin and ok+aiin are "STEM + case-marker" units rather than prepositions.

---

### qokaiin (freq=255) and qotaiin (freq=79) -- qo- prefixed forms

The **qo-** prefix is extremely productive and appears to be a determiner or demonstrative. Thus:
- qokaiin = qo + k + aiin
- qotaiin = qo + t + aiin

These could represent "that/this [thing] for" or "[the] k-thing (genitive marker)".

## 3. daiin Deep Analysis

### Pattern: X daiin Y ("X of Y")

| Metric | Count | Percentage |
|--------|-------|------------|
| Between two words (X daiin Y) | 424 | 63% |
| Line-initial (daiin Y) | 146 | 22% |
| Line-final (X daiin) | 104 | 15% |
| Total | 674 | 100% |

The 63% "between two words" rate strongly supports a genitive/possessive reading where daiin connects a possessor to a possessed noun.

### What precedes daiin (possessor/head noun)

The most common word preceding daiin is **chol** (26 times). Other frequent predecessors:
- chol (26), ol (10), chey (9), qokeey (9), chor (7), chy (7), cheey (7), shol (6), cthol (5), chedy (5)

These are dominated by **ch-** and **sh-** initial words, which are believed to represent plant parts or herbal terminology. The pattern "chol daiin" = "leaf/plant of" makes excellent sense in an herbal manuscript.

### What follows daiin (possessed/dependent noun)

- shey (13), chey (13), cthy (8), chy (8), cthor (7), or (7), ol (7), al (7), chckhy (6), chor (6), chedy (6)

Again dominated by content words, with occasional function words (or, ol, al).

### Comparison with Latin "de" / Italian "di"

In Latin herbal texts, "de" + noun = "concerning/of [plant]" is standard. Italian "di" works similarly. The distribution of daiin matches this pattern:
- Appears between noun phrases (63%)
- Frequently preceded by plant-related words (chol, shol, chor)
- Evenly distributed across sections (universal function word)
- Very high frequency (#1 word) -- "of" is typically among the most frequent words in any language

## 4. Bare "aiin" Analysis

| Word | Frequency |
|------|-----------|
| aiin | 613 |
| ain | 145 |

**Bare "aiin" is the 2nd most frequent word in the manuscript.** Its distribution is peculiar:

### The or/s/r + aiin cluster

The single strongest finding is that bare "aiin" is overwhelmingly preceded by:
- **or** (51 times) -- 8.3% of all aiin instances
- **s** (50 times) -- 8.2%
- **r** (29 times) -- 4.7%
- **ar** (29 times) -- 4.7%
- **ol** (23 times) -- 3.8%

Together, these five short words account for **182 of 613 instances (30%)** of "aiin" predecessors.

### Interpretation

This pattern is best explained by one of two hypotheses:

**Hypothesis A: "aiin" = conjunction "and"**
- "or aiin" = "[something]-or and" -- this seems redundant
- "s aiin" would need s to be a word

**Hypothesis B: Word boundary misplacement**
- "or aiin" is really "or-aiin" = oraiin (attested: 29 times as a single word)
- "s aiin" is really "s-aiin" = saiin (attested: 117 times as a single word)
- The transcription sometimes splits what is morphologically a single word

**Hypothesis B is strongly supported** by the fact that oraiin (29), saiin (117), etc. all exist as fused forms. The "bare aiin" may simply be cases where the scribe left more space between prefix and suffix, and transcribers interpreted it as two words.

**However**, the sheer frequency (613 instances) and the section distribution (concentrated in stars/herbal3/recipes, rare in herbal1-2) suggest that bare "aiin" also has independent function in some contexts, possibly as:
- A nominative/absolutive case marker (zero-prefix = no spatial/relational marking)
- A conjunction ("and")
- A verb form or copula

## 5. Short Function Words: ol, or, ar, al

### Frequency and distribution

| Word | Freq | Avg position | Start | Mid | End |
|------|------|-------------|-------|-----|-----|
| ol | 570 | 0.48 | 128 | 339 | 103 |
| or | 365 | 0.46 | 90 | 222 | 53 |
| ar | 433 | 0.56 | 83 | 227 | 120 |
| al | 295 | 0.59 | 40 | 165 | 90 |

### Section distribution reveals two distinct groups

**ol and or** are concentrated in the **pharma section** (f75-f84):
- ol: pharma=249/570 (44%!)
- or: pharma=67/365 (18%), but also recipes=53 (15%)

**ar and al** are concentrated in the **stars section** (f103-f116):
- ar: stars=161/433 (37%), also astro=46 (11%), cosmo=53 (12%)
- al: stars=152/295 (52%!), cosmo=36 (12%)

This is a **critical discovery**: ol/or and ar/al have complementary distributions. They appear in different manuscript sections. This rules out the hypothesis that they are simple variants of the same word.

### Distributional analysis

**ol** (570):
- Preceded by: qokain (15), aiin (13), ol (12), or (10), r (7)
- Followed by: aiin (23!), chey (20), chedy (17), shedy (16), cheey (13)
- The `ol aiin` bigram (23 instances) is very strong -- supports the word-boundary hypothesis above

**or** (365):
- Preceded by: ol (11), s (10), ar (8), daiin (7), aiin (7)
- Followed by: **aiin (51!)**, ol (10), aiiin (10), al (9), ar (9)
- The `or aiin` bigram (51 instances) is **extremely** strong

**ar** (433):
- Preceded by: s (20), ar (12), otar (12), dar (10), r (9), or (9)
- Followed by: **al (31!)**, aiin (29), ar (12), ain (9), or (8)
- The `ar al` bigram (31 instances) and `ar aiin` (29) are very strong

**al** (295):
- Preceded by: **ar (31!)**, aiin (10), or (9), daiin (7), s (6)
- Followed by: ar (8), chedy (6), keey (5), s (5), kar (4)

### Interpretation

The strong bigram patterns reveal systematic pairing:
- **or -> aiin** (51 times): This is the most frequent bigram involving these words
- **ar -> al** (31 times): Second strongest
- **ar -> aiin** (29 times): Third
- **ol -> aiin** (23 times): Fourth

These pairings suggest **ol/or are one functional pair** and **ar/al are another**, where:

**Hypothesis: ol/or = demonstrative/article system** (this/that, the)
- ol = proximal or definite ("this," "the")
- or = distal or indefinite ("that," "a")
- Their concentration in pharma (instructional text) fits: "take the [ol] root, add a [or] measure"

**Hypothesis: ar/al = case-marking particles or number markers**
- ar al appearing together (31 times!) suggests they form a grammatical unit
- Their concentration in astrological/cosmological texts suggests they may relate to quantities or celestial terminology
- ar -> aiin (29 times) suggests ar can also take the case suffix

**Alternative: Sandhi variants (same stem, different phonological context)**
- o- stem: ol (before l-initial words), or (before r-initial or vowel-initial words)
- a- stem: al (before l-initial), ar (before r-initial)
- Evidence AGAINST this: They have very different section distributions, which sandhi variants should not have

## 8. Summary of Major Findings

### Finding 1: The -aiin morpheme is the core grammatical marker
With 3,747+ tokens across 556 word types, -aiin (and its variant -ain) is the single most important morpheme in the manuscript. It functions as a case/relation marker that can be prefixed with consonantal elements to create different grammatical relationships.

### Finding 2: daiin = "of" is now very well supported
The distributional evidence (63% between content words, "chol daiin" as the strongest bigram, universal section distribution, #1 frequency) makes this the highest-confidence function word identification.

### Finding 3: The prefix system is fully agglutinative
Every prefix (d-, s-, k-, r-, t-) combines with every suffix (-aiin, -ain, -ol, -or, -al, -ar, -air, -am, -an). This produces a regular paradigm of ~10 forms per prefix.

### Finding 4: ol/or and ar/al are two different functional pairs
Despite surface similarity, they have dramatically different section distributions:
- ol/or: concentrated in pharma (recipes/instructions)
- ar/al: concentrated in stars/astro/cosmo (astronomical text)
They cannot be simple sandhi variants of each other.

### Finding 5: Bare "aiin" may be a segmentation artifact
The extremely strong bigrams "or aiin" (51x) and "s aiin" (50x) suggest that many instances of "bare aiin" are actually morphological units whose prefix was separated by transcription conventions. True bare "aiin" may be rarer than the 613 count suggests.

### Finding 6: ch- and sh- are content morphemes, not function prefixes
chol (353), chor (202), chey (506), shol (165), shor (85), shey (337) are among the highest-frequency words. Their concentration in herbal sections and their role as arguments to daiin ("chol daiin" = "leaf of") confirms they are content words (nouns for plant parts), not grammatical particles.

## 9. Revised Morphological Model

```
WORD = (DETERMINER)? + (PREFIX) + STEM + (SUFFIX)

Where:
  DETERMINER = qo- ("the/that")
  PREFIX = consonant cluster (content morpheme)
  STEM = -o-, -e-, -ee-, etc. (vowel pattern = word class?)
  SUFFIX = -l, -r, -n, -m, -y, -dy, etc. (grammatical ending)

FUNCTION WORD = (CASE_PREFIX) + aiin/ain
  CASE_PREFIX = d ("of"), s ("in"), k ("for"), r ("from"), t ("when"), etc.

COMBINED FORM = (CASE_PREFIX) + (DETERMINER_SUFFIX)
  e.g., d + ar = dar ("of the")
        s + ol = sol ("in this")
```

This model accounts for the observed combinatorial explosion of word types while maintaining a small set of productive morphemes.
