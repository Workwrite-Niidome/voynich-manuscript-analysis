# Antidotarium Nicolai vs. Voynich Manuscript Recipe Section: Detailed Comparison

## Date of Research: 2026-04-10

---

## 1. The Antidotarium Nicolai: Overview

### Identity
- **Full title**: Antidotarium Nicolai (also called Antidotarium parvum)
- **Author**: Attributed to Nicolaus Salernitanus (Nicholas of Salerno)
- **Date**: Late 11th or early 12th century
- **Origin**: Schola Medica Salernitana (School of Salerno), southern Italy
- **Language**: Latin
- **Reputation**: Called "without doubt one of the most influential medical texts in medieval literature", "the essential pharmacopeia of the Middle Ages", and "the bible of medieval practical pharmacy"
- **Relationship to Antidotarium magnum**: The AN is a curated selection of the most commonly used recipes from the much larger 11th-century *Antidotarium magnum*, reduced in quantity and standardized in measurement

### Historical Reach
- Official pharmaceutical guide in Naples and Sicily by end of 12th century
- Textbook at University of Paris by 1270
- Textbook at Montpellier by 1309
- Used in Ypres by late 13th century
- Standard reference at every Italian pharmacy and medical school, including Padua, through the 15th century
- First mentioned in 1244 (Vincent of Beauvais, *Speculum Naturale*)
- All known manuscript copies date from mid-13th century or later

### Relationship to Circa Instans
The *Circa Instans* (by Platearius of Salerno) is the companion text to the AN. Where the AN covers **compound medicines** (composita), the *Circa Instans* covers **simples** (simplicia) -- individual ingredients and their properties. The *Circa Instans* describes ~270 natural substances organized alphabetically. Together, they formed the core pharmaceutical curriculum.

## 2. AN Recipe Count vs. Voynich Recipe Count

### AN Recipe Count by Manuscript Version
| Version Type | Recipe Count |
|---|---|
| Oldest versions | ~115 recipes |
| Most common versions | ~150 recipes |
| Later expanded versions | up to 175 recipes |

### Voynich Recipe Section Count
- **Starred paragraphs in f103r-f116v**: ~282-347 entries (estimates vary)
- **Best estimate from structural analysis**: ~282 starred paragraphs extracted
- **Original source estimate** (accounting for 4 missing pages): 300-400 entries

### Comparison
The Voynich recipe section contains significantly MORE entries than a standard AN (~282 vs. ~150). This rules out a simple 1:1 correspondence with the AN.

However, several scenarios remain viable:
1. **The Voynich recipes = AN + additional recipes** from other sources (local additions were common in manuscript tradition)
2. **The Voynich recipes subdivide AN recipes**: Some complex AN recipes (like Esdra with 40 ingredients) could be split into multiple shorter entries
3. **The Voynich uses a different granularity**: AN recipes are entire compound preparations; Voynich entries may be individual ingredient instructions within a recipe
4. **The Voynich is not the AN** but a related pharmaceutical text (Ricettario, local apothecary formulary)

### Abbreviated AN Hypothesis
There is no known abbreviated AN with exactly ~60 recipes. The minimum known count is ~115 recipes. However, individual apothecary shops and medical practitioners are known to have kept personal extracts of the AN containing only the recipes they used most frequently. Such personal extracts could plausibly contain 55-60 recipes.

## 3. AN Recipe Structure

### Standard Recipe Format
Each AN recipe follows this structure:

```
1. [RECIPE NAME] — Etymological explanation of the name
2. [THERAPEUTIC SECTION ("laus")] — Indications, what conditions it treats
3. "Recipe" (℞) — Opening imperative ("Take")
4. [INGREDIENT LIST] — Ingredients with quantities, using "ana" for equal parts
5. [PREPARATION] — Brief instructions (often minimal)
6. [DOSAGE & ADMINISTRATION] — How much to give and how
7. [PHARMACEUTICAL FORM] — What form the medicine takes
```

### Example: Athanasia (from BNF Latin 6823, 14th c.)

```
Dissinterias subvenit athanasia et sanguinis fluxum
[= Athanasia relieves dysentery and blood flow]

R[ecipe] [cinnamomum, cassia] ʒ.iii. et grana vi.
croci, iusquiami, [styrax], fu[...], betonica, apium,
daucus, anise, opium ān̄ ʒ.i. et Ṡ. et grana iii.
spica, castor, myrrha, haematites, sanguis draconis,
corallium rubrum, mumia, costus, bolus, consolida,
auripigmentum, asarum, mandragora, iris, polium,
baccae lauri, meu, piper longum, piper album,
petroselinum ʒ.v.
mel quod sufficit.
[= honey as needed]

Dosis: ʒ.iii. cum suco plantaginis
[= 3 drachmas with plantain juice]

vi. pars confecta est libra i.
[= the sixth part prepared is one pound]
```

### Example: Esdra (from BNF Latin 6823, f189v-190r)

```
Esdra misture paralisi maxime curat.
[= The Esdra mixture especially cures paralysis]

Esdra dicta est quia esdras propheta eam primo invenit.
[= It is called Esdra because the prophet Esdra first discovered it]
Vale proprie melanconicis timidis et quibus malus est appetitus...
[= It is good especially for melancholics, the timid, and those with poor appetite...]

Recipe alipte muscate mirre zuchari foliorum rute apii seminis
camedreius fenu greci an drachma i et scrupulum i et granum i.
[= Take musk alipte, myrrh, sugar, rue leaves, celery seeds,
germander, fenugreek, each 1 drachm, 1 scruple, and 1 grain]

ellebori seminis rute agreste eupatorii balaustie
an drachma i et grana vii et S.
[= hellebore, wild rue seeds, eupatorium, balaustia,
each 1 drachm and 7.5 grains]

cinnamomum cariofili spica curcuma fructus rose capparis
abrotanum pulegium piper nigrum petroselinum seminis dactili
an scrupula ii et S et grana iiii.
[= cinnamon, cloves, spike, curcuma, rose fruits, capers,
southernwood, pennyroyal, black pepper, parsley, date seeds,
each 2.5 scruples and 4 grains]

[... continues with ~40 total ingredients in grouped quantities ...]

mel quod sufficit datur in sero cum uino calido a modum auellane.
[= honey as needed, given in the evening with warm wine, the size of a hazelnut]
```

### Key Structural Features
1. **Opening**: Recipe name + etymology + therapeutic indication BEFORE the ingredient list
2. **"Recipe" (℞)**: Imperative "Take" marks the transition from description to formulation
3. **Ingredient grouping**: Ingredients sharing the same quantity are listed together, followed by "an[a]" + measurement
4. **"Ana" (an̄, ān̄)**: "Equal parts" -- groups of ingredients all at the same dose
5. **Closing**: Dosage amount + vehicle (juice, wine, honey) + administration route

## 4. AN Measurement System (Apothecaries' Weights)

The AN is credited with introducing the **Salernian apothecaries' weight system**, which became the European standard.

### Conversion Table
| Unit | Latin | Symbol | Equivalent |
|---|---|---|---|
| Pound | libra | lb, ℔ | = 12 unciae |
| Ounce | uncia | ℥ | = 8 drachmae |
| Drachm | drachma | ʒ | = 3 scrupula |
| Scruple | scrupulum | ℈ | = 20 grana |
| Grain | granum | gr | (base unit) |

### Full conversion: 1 libra = 12 ℥ = 96 ʒ = 288 ℈ = 5,760 gr

### Most Common Quantities in AN Recipes
- **ʒ.i.** (1 drachm) -- most frequent single quantity
- **ʒ.ii.** (2 drachms) -- very common
- **ʒ.iii.** (3 drachms) -- common for dosages
- **an̄ ʒ.i.** (equal parts, 1 drachm each) -- extremely frequent for grouped ingredients
- **℈.i.** (1 scruple) -- for smaller quantities
- **q.s.** (*quod sufficit*, "as much as needed") -- for honey, wine, water
- **ad modum avellanae** ("the size of a hazelnut") -- for dosage

### Abbreviation Patterns
- **i, ii, iii, iiii** (or iv) for numerals
- **S** or **Ṡ** for *semis* (half)
- **an̄** for *ana* (equal parts)
- **q.s.** for *quod sufficit*
- **R** or **℞** for *Recipe*

## 5. AN Pharmaceutical Forms

The AN describes 8 pharmaceutical forms:

| Form | Latin | Description |
|---|---|---|
| Electuaries | electuaria | Soft pastes mixed with honey/sugar |
| Morsels | morsuli | Small cakes or lozenges |
| Pills | pilulae | Round solid doses |
| Troches | trochisci | Tablet-like pressed forms |
| Syrups | sirupi | Sweet liquid preparations |
| Plasters | emplastra | External applications |
| Ointments | unguenta | Topical greasy preparations |
| Oils | olea | Oil-based preparations |

### Distribution (from scholarly analysis of 142 remedies)
- **Electuaries**: At least 75 out of 142 remedies (~53%) -- the dominant form
- **Emplastra**: Only 3 in the AN
- The majority of AN recipes are internal medicines (electuaries, pills, syrups)

## 8. Structural Comparison: AN vs. Voynich Recipe Section

### 8.1 Opening Formula

| Feature | Antidotarium Nicolai | Voynich Recipe Section |
|---|---|---|
| Recipe delimiter | Red/decorated initial letter | Star symbol in margin |
| Recipe name | First element, alphabetically ordered | First word (gallows-initial), highly diverse (~200+ unique) |
| Etymology/explanation | After name, before ingredients | No apparent equivalent |
| "Recipe" (℞) command | Explicit word marking ingredient list start | No separate "Recipe" word; star may serve this function |
| First word pattern | Recipe name (variable) | Gallows-initial word (variable, 87.9% start with p/t/k/f) |

**Critical parallel**: Both the AN and the Voynich recipe section open each entry with a **unique identifier** (recipe name / unique first word), NOT with a repeated verb like "Take." The star symbol in the Voynich may functionally replace the "℞" marker.

### 8.2 Internal Structure

| Feature | Antidotarium Nicolai | Voynich Recipe Section |
|---|---|---|
| Ingredient grouping | Multiple ingredients per line, grouped by shared quantity | Possible grouping (qo- prefix words cluster) |
| "Ana" (equal parts) | Very frequent, groups 3-10 ingredients at same dose | No identified equivalent yet |
| Quantity notation | ʒ.i., ℈.ii., gr.vii. etc. | No clear numeric/quantity system identified |
| Connectors | et (and), cum (with), in (in) | ar, ol, al (frequent short words) |
| Preparation verbs | tere (grind), misce (mix), cola (strain) | Words ending in -aiin (possible verb infinitives) |
| Closing formula | Dosage + vehicle (cum vino, cum aqua) | Diverse endings, -aiin and -am patterns |

### 8.3 Entry Length

| Text | Average Entry Length | Range |
|---|---|---|
| AN short recipe | ~30-50 words | 20-80 words |
| AN complex recipe (Esdra) | ~200+ words | Up to 300 |
| Voynich short recipe | ~10-15 words | 8-25 words |
| Voynich long recipe | ~30-50 words | Up to ~60 |

**Critical difference**: Voynich recipe entries are consistently SHORTER than AN recipes. This could indicate:
1. Heavy abbreviation or encoding compression
2. The Voynich records only the ingredient list (omitting therapeutic description and dosage)
3. The Voynich uses a different source text with simpler recipes
4. Each Voynich entry is a sub-component of a larger recipe

### 8.4 Alphabetical Organization

| Feature | AN | Voynich |
|---|---|---|
| Alphabetical ordering | Yes, strictly alphabetical by recipe name | **Unknown** -- first words show no obvious alphabetical pattern |
| Sequential organization | A through Z (with some gaps) | Possibly organized by therapeutic category or another principle |

The **lack of alphabetical ordering** in the Voynich recipe section is a significant point of divergence from the AN. If the Voynich were a direct copy of the AN, we would expect alphabetical order. The recipes may instead be ordered by:
- Body part (head to toe, common in medieval medicine)
- Pharmaceutical form (electuaries, then pills, then syrups)
- Source plant (matching the herbal section order)
- Therapeutic category (fevers, wounds, digestion, etc.)

## 9. The "Dia-" Prefix and Voynich Word Patterns

### AN Pattern
A large proportion of AN recipe names begin with "Dia-" (Greek, "made from"):
- Diacalamentum, Diacapparis, Diacastoreum, Diaciminum, Diacodion, Diacostum, Diacurcuma, Diadragantum, Diagalanga, Dialacca, Dialibanum, Diamanna, Diamargariton, Diamastic, Diantos, Diapapaver, Diaprassium, Diaprunis, Diarebarbarum, Diasatirion, Diasene, Diatrion pipereon, Diaziuziberos

At least 23 out of ~150 recipes (~15%) start with "Dia-".

### Voynich Pattern
The Voynich recipe section shows a dominant first-word prefix pattern:
- **p- initial**: 54.6% of recipes
  - pch-: 21.6%
  - po- (especially pol-): 21.3%
  - pa-: 5.3%
- **t- initial**: 21.3% of recipes

If the Voynich is encoding AN-type recipe names:
- The p- prefix could encode "Dia-" or another recurrent prefix
- The sub-patterns (pch-, po-, pa-) could encode different second syllables of recipe names
- But the 54.6% frequency of p- far exceeds the 15% frequency of "Dia-" in the AN

More likely, the gallows-initial pattern reflects the cipher/encoding system rather than a direct letter-for-letter correspondence with AN names.

## 10. "Ana" and Voynich Repetitive Patterns

### AN "Ana" Usage
In the AN, "ana" (abbreviated an̄ or ān̄) is one of the most frequent words, appearing in virtually every recipe. It means "of each, equal parts" and groups ingredients:

```
croci, iusquiami, styracis, betonica, apii ān̄ ʒ.i. et Ṡ.
[= saffron, henbane, styrax, betony, celery, EACH 1.5 drachms]
```

### Voynich Candidate for "Ana"
The Voynich recipe section shows several highly repetitive patterns:
- **qokeey.qokeedy** -- extremely frequent pair
- **chedy.qokeey** -- strongest bigram (19 occurrences)
- **shedy.qokeey** -- 11 occurrences

If any Voynich word corresponds to "ana," it would need to:
1. Appear very frequently (dozens of times per page)
2. Appear between ingredient groups
3. Often be followed by quantity indicators

The words **chedy** and **shedy** are candidates -- they appear repeatedly in medial positions, separating content word groups. Alternatively, the repetitive qo- prefix itself could encode the "ana" concept (marking each ingredient as "equal part").

## 11. AN Illustrations

### AN Manuscript Illustration Tradition
Most AN manuscripts are **text-only**. Decorative elements include:
- **Illuminated initials**: Red/blue decorated capitals at recipe starts
- **Red penwork flourishes**: Marking text divisions
- **Rubrication**: Recipe names in red ink
- Small miniatures (rare, mainly in deluxe copies)

**No AN manuscript is known to contain systematic botanical or pharmaceutical illustrations alongside recipes.** This is because the AN describes compound medicines (composita), not individual plants (simplicia).

### Voynich "Pharmaceutical" Section (f87r-f102v)
The Voynich pharmaceutical section (distinct from the recipe/stars section) contains:
- Drawings of containers/jars in red, blue, green
- Small plant parts (leaves, roots) arranged near containers
- Over 100 different plant depictions

### Comparison
The Voynich pharmaceutical section with its jar illustrations has **no parallel in the AN tradition**. However, it does parallel other Italian pharmaceutical manuscripts where apothecary jars are depicted. The recipe/stars section (f103r-f116v) is text-only with star markers, which is structurally closer to the AN format of text-only entries with decorated initials.

## 12. If the Voynich IS Related to the AN

### Scenario A: The Voynich recipe section is an encoded AN subset
- **Expected**: ~115-150 entries, alphabetical order, identifiable "Recipe" word, "ana" equivalent, quantity notation
- **Observed**: ~282 entries, no alphabetical order, no "Recipe" word, diverse openings
- **Assessment**: UNLIKELY as a direct copy. Too many entries, wrong organization.

### Scenario B: The Voynich recipe section uses AN as a structural template
- The recipe FORMAT (name + ingredients + preparation) could follow AN conventions
- But the CONTENT could be different (local recipes, vernacular adaptations)
- The star marker replaces the decorated initial/℞ symbol
- **Assessment**: PLAUSIBLE. The structural parallels are real.

### Scenario C: The Voynich is a local apothecary formulary influenced by AN tradition
- A 15th-century Italian pharmacist trained on the AN would structure their personal notebook similarly
- Personal formularies typically contained:
  - AN recipes they used daily (perhaps 40-60)
  - Local/regional recipes not in the AN
  - Simplified versions of complex AN recipes
  - Personal modifications and notes
- Total entries could easily reach 200-300
- **Assessment**: MOST PLAUSIBLE. This explains both the structural parallels AND the divergences.

### Scenario D: The Voynich recipe section is NOT pharmaceutical
- The 300+ entries with their short, formulaic structure could also match:
  - Color/pigment recipes (as proposed by Cipher Mysteries, citing Jean le Begue's MS 6741 with 359 recipes)
  - Alchemical processes
  - Astrological instructions
- **Assessment**: Cannot be ruled out, but the connection to the pharmaceutical/herbal sections argues for medical content.

## 16. Conclusions

### Strong Parallels
1. **Entry-level structure**: Both texts organize content as discrete recipe entries, each with a unique identifier, ingredient content, and instructions
2. **Opening pattern**: Both begin entries with a variable name/identifier, not a fixed verb
3. **Delimiter system**: Star = decorated initial (functional equivalent)
4. **Text-only format**: The recipe section is purely textual (no illustrations), matching AN tradition
5. **Companion herbal**: Both the AN and the Voynich have a separate section for simples/plants (Circa Instans / Herbal section), paralleling the composita/simplicia division
6. **Italian pharmaceutical context**: The AN was THE standard text in 15th-century Italian pharmacy, exactly the context proposed for the Voynich

### Significant Divergences
1. **Entry count**: ~282 vs. ~150 (Voynich has roughly double)
2. **No alphabetical order**: The AN is strictly alphabetical; the Voynich is not
3. **Much shorter entries**: Voynich recipes average 10-30 words vs. AN's 30-200+
4. **No visible quantity system**: The AN's elaborate weight notation (ʒ, ℈, gr, ana) has no clear Voynich equivalent
5. **No identifiable "Recipe" word**: The AN always has an explicit ℞; the Voynich does not

### Most Likely Relationship
The Voynich recipe section is **NOT a direct copy of the AN**, but it appears to follow the **structural conventions of the AN tradition**. The most plausible scenario is that the Voynich recipe section represents a **personal apothecary formulary** or **local pharmacological compilation** created by someone trained in the Italian pharmaceutical tradition where the AN was the foundational text. The author used the same structural conventions (entry delimiter, name-first organization, ingredient lists, preparation instructions) but recorded a different -- possibly more extensive, possibly more localized -- set of recipes in an encoded form.

3. **Bigram analysis**: The AN has characteristic bigrams (ana + numeral, Recipe + ingredient) -- do Voynich bigrams cluster in similar positional patterns?
4. **Cross-reference with Circa Instans**: If the Voynich herbal section corresponds to Circa Instans simples, then ingredient words from the herbal should reappear in the recipe section -- test this cross-section vocabulary overlap
5. **Search for Italian vernacular ANs**: The AN was translated into Middle English, Dutch, and other vernaculars -- search for 15th-century Italian vernacular translations that might have simplified the recipe structure
