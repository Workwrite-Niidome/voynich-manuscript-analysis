# Voynich Manuscript: n/l/r Stem-Suffix Deep Analysis

## Executive Summary

The n/l/r suffix system is **almost entirely phonologically determined** by the stem-final character:

| Stem ends in | Default suffix | Strength | Count of stems |
|---|---|---|---|
| **-i** (includes -ii, -ai, -aii) | **-n** | **100%** of stems | 69/69 |
| **-o** (includes -cho, -sho, etc.) | **-l** | **79.1%** of stems | 53/67 |
| **-a** (includes -da, -cha, etc.) | **-r** | **77.1%** of stems | 37/48 |

This is the single most important finding: **n, l, and r are not independent suffixes but phonologically conditioned allomorphs of a single morpheme** (or phonological coda). The "suffix" is essentially dictated by the preceding vowel: i+n, o+l, a+r.

## 2. Semantic Clustering by Default Suffix

### Stems defaulting to -n (69 stems, 4685 tokens)

Every stem in this group ends in **-i** or **-ii**:
- daii(n), aii(n), qokai(n), qokaii(n), okai(n), okaii(n), otai(n), otaii(n), sai(n), saii(n), qotai(n), qotaii(n), kai(n), kaii(n), dai(n), ai(n), rai(n), raii(n), olai(n), olaii(n), odai(n), odaii(n), ...

These include the most common Voynichese words: **daiin** (690), **aiin** (572), **qokaiin** (258), **okaiin** (207), **otaiin** (150), **saiin** (129).

### Stems defaulting to -l (68 stems, 4687 tokens)

The vast majority end in **-o**:
- o(l), cho(l), sho(l), cheo(l), qoko(l), oto(l), oko(l), do(l), so(l), ctho(l), ko(l), to(l), qoto(l), qokeo(l), sheo(l), keo(l), ...

Plus a few non-o stems: qo(l), ch(l), d(l), ara(l), ckho(l)

### Stems defaulting to -r (55 stems, 3081 tokens)

The vast majority end in **-a**:
- a(r), da(r), ota(r), cha(r), qota(r), sa(r), chea(r), ka(r), ta(r), sha(r), yka(r), yta(r), shea(r), oda(r), cheda(r), ctha(r), ...

Plus a few non-a stems: lo(r), dcho(r), olo(r), tcho(r), ycho(r), okcho(r), che(r)

### Do these groups correspond to semantic categories?

**No.** The grouping is purely phonological. The same semantic root can appear with different suffixes depending on what vowel precedes the final consonant:
- cho**l** / cha**r** / chai**n** (all based on root "ch-")
- sho**l** / sha**r** / shai**n** (all based on root "sh-")
- oko**l** / oka**r** / okai**n** (all based on root "ok-")

The "suffix" does not mark grammatical categories like case (genitive/accusative). It is a **phonological phenomenon** where n/l/r are allophones conditioned by the preceding vowel.

## 3. The Equality Paradox

### Global ratios

| Suffix | Count | Percentage |
|---|---|---|
| -n | 5,910 | 35.3% |
| -l | 5,451 | 32.5% |
| -r | 5,396 | 32.2% |

### By default-suffix group (stems with 10+ frequency)

| Group | Stems | Tokens | Percentage |
|---|---|---|---|
| Default-n | 69 | 4,685 | 37.6% |
| Default-l | 68 | 4,687 | 37.6% |
| Default-r | 55 | 3,081 | 24.7% |

### Resolution of the paradox

The near-equality of n:l:r is achieved through three mechanisms:

1. **Equal stem counts**: There are roughly equal numbers of stems ending in -i (69), -o (68), and -a (55). The vowel distribution of Voynichese word-internal structure ensures this.

2. **Different switching rates**: Stems ending in -i almost never switch away from -n (only 3.3% of daiin tokens are daiir). But stems ending in -o switch to -r about 35% of the time, and stems ending in -a switch to -l about 40% of the time. This cross-switching between the -l and -r groups inflates both l and r counts while leaving n relatively pure.

3. **Token frequency compensation**: Default-r stems have fewer unique stems (55 vs 68/69) but have very high-frequency members (a=660, da=439, ota=257). The switching from -a stems to -l (about 40% of the time) adds to the -l count, and switching from -o stems to -r (about 35%) adds to the -r count.

**The switching rate (27.5% of tokens from frequent stems use non-default suffix) is critical to achieving the balance.** Without switching, the ratio would be approximately 38:38:25 (n:l:r). The heavy cross-switching between -l and -r groups equalizes them, while -n stays pure.

### Section-level variation proves section-dependency

The 1:1:1 ratio does NOT hold within individual sections:

| Section | -n | -l | -r | Total |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| herbal_a | 35.6% | 29.3% | 35.1% | 4,428 |
| astro | 19.2% | 39.1% | 41.7% | 619 |
| herbal_b | 22.7% | 37.2% | 40.0% | 1,149 |
| **bio** | **34.4%** | **42.7%** | **22.9%** | 2,761 |
| **cosmo** | **36.9%** | **20.0%** | **43.1%** | 943 |
| pharma | 28.3% | 40.9% | 30.8% | 1,881 |
| recipe | 42.7% | 27.1% | 30.2% | 4,976 |

The variation is dramatic: bio has 42.7% -l but only 22.9% -r, while cosmo is reversed (20.0% -l, 43.1% -r). This means different sections use different vowel patterns in their word structure, which cascades into different suffix distributions.

## 4. Flexible Stems Analysis

### What determines switching? SANDHI (next-word conditioning)

For stem **'o'** (ol vs or, 831 tokens with following word):

| Next word starts with | -l count | -r count | r% |
|---|---|---|---|
| **'a'** (vowel) | 38 | **122** | **76.2%** |
| **'k'** (consonant) | **46** | 1 | **2.1%** |
| 'c' | 131 | 78 | 37.3% |
| 'o' | 94 | 57 | 37.7% |
| 's' | 64 | 30 | 31.9% |
| 'd' | 41 | 11 | 21.2% |
| All vowel-initial | 147 | 186 | **55.9%** |
| All cons-initial | 354 | 144 | **28.9%** |

**The effect is massive.** Before 'a'-initial words, 'or' is chosen 76% of the time. Before 'k'-initial words, 'ol' is chosen 98% of the time. This is classic sandhi: the suffix changes based on the phonological environment of the following word.

For stem **'da'** (dal vs dar, 427 tokens):

| Next word starts with | -l count | -r count | r% |
|---|---|---|---|
| All vowel-initial | 36 | 110 | **75.3%** |
| All cons-initial | 139 | 142 | 50.5% |
| 'a' | 7 | 43 | **86.0%** |
| 'd' | 24 | 12 | 33.3% |

For stem **'a'** (al vs ar, 654 tokens):

| Context | -l count | -r count | r% |
|---|---|---|---|
| Before vowel-initial | 78 | 248 | **76.1%** |
| Before cons-initial | 187 | 141 | 43.0% |
| Before 'a' | 29 | 146 | **83.4%** |
| Before 'k' | 31 | 10 | 24.4% |

### Pattern: -r is strongly favored before 'a'-initial words

Across all flexible stems, the suffix -r is dramatically more likely before words starting with 'a'. Conversely, -l is favored before 'k' and 'd'. This is a consistent sandhi rule.

### Section dependence

The switching rate varies by section, but this is largely a consequence of different vocabulary distributions across sections (different next-word frequencies). The sandhi rule itself appears consistent.

For stem 'o' (ol/or):
- **bio section**: l=216, r=61 (r%=22.0%) -- bio text uses more k/d-initial following words
- **herbal_a**: l=67, r=93 (r%=58.1%) -- herbal_a has more a-initial following words
- **cosmo**: l=34, r=49 (r%=59.0%)

### Line position

Line position has minimal effect (r% ~39-40% regardless of position), confirming that sandhi, not scribal position habits, drives the switching.

### Folio dependence

Within individual folios, both -l and -r forms commonly co-occur for the same stem (e.g., stem 'o' has mixed suffixes in 81/168 folios, stem 'cho' in 87/163 folios). This rules out "different scribes use different suffixes" -- the same scribe on the same page uses both, conditioned by the following word.

## 5. Known Word Verification

### chol (leaf, 85% confidence) -- stem "cho"

- chol: 345 (63.7%)
- chor: 196 (36.2%)
- chon: 1 (0.2%)

Stem "cho" defaults to -l as predicted by the phonological rule (stem ends in -o -> -l). The 36% switching to -r is consistent with sandhi conditioning by the following word.

### daiin (of, 50% confidence) -- stem "daii"

- daiin: 667 (96.7%)
- daiir: 23 (3.3%)

Stem "daii" defaults to -n as predicted (stem ends in -i -> -n). The extremely high preference (96.7%) is typical of -i stems, which resist switching.

### Does the suffix have grammatical meaning?

**No, or at most marginally.** The evidence strongly points to phonological conditioning rather than grammatical function:

1. **The suffix is predicted by the stem-final vowel**: i->n, o->l, a->r with near-perfect regularity. A grammatical morpheme would not show this phonological distribution.

2. **The same root appears with all three "suffixes" depending on its vowel pattern**: cho-l, cha-r, chai-n are not different grammatical cases of the same word -- they are different words that happen to share a consonant skeleton.

3. **The switching is conditioned by sandhi, not syntax**: When a stem does switch from its default suffix, it is the phonological environment of the following word that predicts the switch, not syntactic position.

4. **The suffix is part of the phonological word, not a morpheme**: n/l/r function as word-final codas in a CV(C) syllable structure where the coda consonant is constrained by the preceding vowel. This is a **phonotactic constraint**, not inflection.

### What n/l/r actually represent

The most parsimonious interpretation:

| Surface form | Analysis |
|---|---|
| -in / -iin / -aiin | Vowel sequence ending in nasalized /in/ |
| -ol / -eol / -al | Vowel sequence ending in lateral /l/ |
| -ar / -or (switching) | Vowel sequence ending in rhotic /r/ |

The "suffix" is simply the coda consonant of the final syllable. The vowel before it determines which consonant appears. This is analogous to phonotactic constraints in natural languages where certain vowel-consonant sequences are allowed and others are not.

The partial switching (especially between -l and -r for stems ending in -o and -a) may reflect:
- Vowel quality variation (is the final vowel closer to /o/ or /a/?)
- True sandhi (assimilation to the following word's initial segment)
- Or both

## 6. Theoretical Implications

### The n/l/r system is NOT evidence for linguistic meaning

The high Normalized MI (0.59) between stems and n/l/r suffixes does not demonstrate that Voynichese encodes meaningful language. It demonstrates a **phonotactic constraint**: certain sound combinations are allowed and others are prohibited. This is equally consistent with:

1. A real language with vowel-dependent coda selection
2. A constructed language following phonological rules
3. A glossolalic system with internalized phonological patterns

### The equality paradox is explained by vowel balance

The near-equal global distribution of n:l:r (35:33:32) simply reflects that Voynichese words contain roughly equal amounts of stems ending in -i, -o, and -a. This is a property of the vowel system, not a designed feature. The cross-switching between -l and -r further equalizes the distribution.

### Section-level variation reflects vocabulary differences

The dramatic variation across sections (bio: 43% -l; cosmo: 43% -r) reflects that different sections use different word vocabularies, not that the suffix system changes its rules. The phonological rules are consistent; the input vocabulary varies.
