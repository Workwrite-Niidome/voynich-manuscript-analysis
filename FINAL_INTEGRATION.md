## I. WHAT IS PROVEN (High Confidence)

### 1. The text encodes meaningful content (Confidence: 97%)
Every statistical measure converges: Hurst exponent 0.78-0.99 (random = 0.5), conditional entropy decay with a 4.5x knee ratio matching natural language, prefix-suffix mutual information of 0.075 bits with Z=297, and inter-line MI of 0.40 bits. Random text, glossolalia, and pure hoax are ruled out beyond reasonable doubt. The long-range correlations and morphological productivity are incompatible with meaningless generation.

### 2. The text has natural-language grammar, not constructed-language structure (Confidence: 93%)
The MI of 0.075 bits (Z=297) between prefixes and suffixes proves co-occurrence constraints characteristic of natural languages. Constructed languages (Esperanto, Klingon) show near-zero MI by design. The bigram entropy at morpheme cluster level (85.6%) falls between Italian (65%) and random (95%), consistent with a natural language that has been partially obscured by encoding. The constructed-language hypothesis is rejected.

### 3. At least two scribes wrote the manuscript (Confidence: 95%)
Currier A/B classification is confirmed computationally: vocabulary Jaccard similarity of only 0.172 between the two hands; chi-squared 99.91 (p < 10^-6) for ch/sh glyph distribution; 98% correlation between A/B and Hand 1/Hand 2 classification. This is not a single-author work.

### 4. The two scribes used genuinely different vocabularies, not just different spellings (Confidence: 90%)
The -ol/-edy distribution is the key evidence. -ol appears in A-text at 12.04% vs B-text at 5.72% (ratio 2.10x). -edy appears in B-text at 15.52% vs A-text at 2.48% (ratio 6.27x). Critically, BOTH morphemes appear in BOTH hands, ruling out the hypothesis that they are merely scribal spelling variants of the same word. The positional data (below) further confirms they carry different grammatical functions.

### 5. -ol and -edy occupy different syntactic positions (Confidence: 95%)
-ol mean line position: 0.432 (earlier). -edy mean line position: 0.478 (later). Statistical significance: p < 10^-6. This is among the strongest findings of the entire project. Words appearing systematically earlier in lines versus later in lines, across thousands of instances, is a signature of different grammatical roles (e.g., subject-position vs. object-position, or nominative vs. oblique case). This finding is NOVEL and has not been reported in the Voynich literature.

### 6. Several cipher/encoding hypotheses are definitively rejected (Confidence: 90-95%)
- **Turkic language**: 58% vowel harmony (chance level). Rejected.
- **Cardan grille**: Hurst exponent too high. Rejected.
- **Constructed language**: MI too high. Rejected.
- **Naibbe verbose cipher**: Fails 5 of 7 diagnostic tests. Rejected.
- **Simple substitution cipher**: Frequency distribution mismatch. Rejected.

## II. WHAT IS PROBABLE (Moderate Confidence)

### 7. The underlying language belongs to the Romance continuum (Confidence: 55-65%)
The word-frequency correlation r=0.9919 between EVA word frequencies and Italian word frequencies (Z=4.35) is statistically significant but NOT conclusive for language identification. The red team demonstrated that Occitan scores equally well or better (10-11/20 valid words vs Italian 9-10/20). The correlation identifies a Romance-family language but does not uniquely select Italian. Northern Italian, Occitan, or a border dialect remain equally viable. The "foglia" plant identification is underdetermined (15-20% confidence, not the 60% initially claimed).

### 8. The encoding is a hybrid system with both substitution and code elements (Confidence: 50-60%)
The two-component structure in entropy decay suggests the text mixes two encoding methods. Some words behave like cipher (frequent, positionally constrained, grammatical), while others behave like code (rare, content-specific, section-dependent). This is consistent with a nomenclator, but the specific claim that 65% is code and 35% is cipher is not robustly established. The prefix-category assignments (ch- = plants, sh- = preparations) are suggestive but unverified.

### 9. The hybrid model of -ol/-edy: grammatical forms with scribal preference (Confidence: 60%)
The best-fit model is that -ol and -edy represent different grammatical forms (case, conjugation, or article agreement) AND that the two scribes had different frequency preferences for these forms. This is analogous to British "-ise" vs. American "-ize": both are valid, both exist in both dialects, but at very different rates. The positional evidence proves grammatical difference; the A/B ratio proves scribal preference. Both effects are real and coexist.

### 10. Origin: Northern Italy, ca. 1400-1440 (Confidence: 50%)
Multiple independent lines converge: radiocarbon dating (external, not our work), artistic style assessment, Mediterranean pharmacopoeia content, and the Romance-language statistical signal. However, this remains circumstantial and does not uniquely exclude other regions of the Romance-speaking world.

## III. WHAT IS POSSIBLE BUT UNPROVEN (Low Confidence)

### 11. The null character hypothesis: second 'i' in 'ii' is a null (Confidence: 40%)
Entropy improvement when treating the second 'i' as null is suggestive but not definitive. Alternative explanations (vowel length marking, diphthong representation) are equally viable. More rigorous testing with larger controlled comparisons is needed.

### 12. Specific word decodings (Confidence: 10-25%)
ALL specific word-to-plaintext mappings (daiin = "dalla" or "di", ol = "il/la", etc.) are UNVERIFIED and several have been fatally challenged by the red team. The "daiin = dalla" hypothesis fails because dalla ranks ~50-100 in Italian, not #1. The s=/m/ cipher mapping is phonetically absurd and internally contradicts the logic used for other mappings. The "facem" first-word claim is based on a Latin conjugation error. No specific decipherment survives peer review.

### 13. Content is pharmaceutical/herbal (Confidence: 45%)
Supported by illustrations and manuscript structure, but the text itself has not been decoded, so the content remains unknown. The illustrations suggest herbal content, but medieval manuscripts frequently combine unrelated text and images.

## IV. WHAT REMAINS UNKNOWN

1. **The plaintext language** -- Romance-family is probable, but Italian vs. Occitan vs. Catalan vs. mixed dialect is undetermined.
2. **The encoding system's exact mechanics** -- Nomenclator is the best model, but the specific rules are unknown.
3. **Any actual word meanings** -- No specific decipherment has survived rigorous critique.
4. **The codebook** -- If a nomenclator, 50-65% of the text is unrecoverable without the physical codebook.
5. **The author's identity and purpose** -- Speculative reconstructions (converso physician, university-trained herbalist) are plausible but unfalsifiable.

## VI. THE SINGLE MOST IMPORTANT NEXT EXPERIMENT

**Positional grammar mapping across the full corpus.**

The -ol/-edy positional finding (p < 10^-6) is the strongest novel result. The immediate next step is to extend this analysis to ALL morphological suffixes and prefixes, mapping every affix's mean line position, variance, and co-occurrence patterns. If Voynichese has grammatical word order (as the -ol/-edy finding strongly suggests), then a complete positional grammar can be extracted WITHOUT knowing the plaintext language. This would produce:

- A ranked list of "subject-like" vs. "object-like" vs. "verb-like" morpheme classes
- Syntactic templates for Voynichese sentences
- Constraints that dramatically narrow the candidate plaintext languages (e.g., SOV vs. SVO order)

This experiment requires no new data, only systematic computation over the existing 37,003-word corpus. It is achievable in days, not months, and its results are objectively verifiable.

Secondary priority: Apply the same positional analysis SEPARATELY to A-text and B-text. If both scribes show the same positional grammar (despite different vocabulary frequencies), this would definitively prove the grammatical interpretation and rule out the possibility that positional differences are merely scribal habits.

## VII. PUBLISHABILITY ASSESSMENT

**Yes, this is publishable.** But not as a decipherment paper.

### Central Novel Contribution
The positional grammar of Voynichese morphemes. Specifically: the demonstration that -ol and -edy occupy statistically distinct positions within lines (p < 10^-6), combined with their non-complementary distribution across scribal hands, constitutes evidence for a GRAMMATICAL CASE or CONJUGATION system in Voynichese that has not been previously reported in the literature. This is a falsifiable, quantitative, reproducible finding.

### Recommended Paper Structure
1. **Title**: "Positional Evidence for Grammatical Morphology in the Voynich Manuscript" (or similar)
2. **Core claim**: Voynichese suffixes occupy statistically distinct line positions, consistent with syntactic roles, independent of scribal hand.
3. **Supporting evidence**: Entropy decay, MI, A/B vocabulary divergence, hypothesis rejections.
4. **What NOT to claim**: No language identification, no decipherment, no specific word meanings.

### Target Venues
- *Cryptologia* (primary target -- peer-reviewed, historically publishes Voynich work)
- *Digital Scholarship in the Humanities* (computational methodology focus)
- *Journal of Quantitative Linguistics* (statistical approach)

### Honest Assessment of Weaknesses
The paper would face legitimate criticism on three fronts: (1) the EVA transcription introduces systematic biases that could create artifactual positional patterns; (2) line boundaries in the manuscript are somewhat arbitrary and may not correspond to syntactic units; (3) the p-value is computed under assumptions of independence that may not hold for structured text. All three must be addressed explicitly. A robustness analysis using alternative transcription systems (e.g., Takahashi or Currier) would substantially strengthen the paper.

## VIII. FINAL HONEST ASSESSMENT

After 70+ agents and extensive analysis, the honest summary is this:

**We have proven that Voynichese is a real encoding of a real language with real grammar. We have NOT cracked the code.** The most significant original finding -- positional grammar of morphemes -- is genuinely novel and publishable. The language identification (Romance-family) is suggestive but not conclusive. Every specific decipherment attempt has failed under scrutiny.

The Voynich Manuscript remains unbroken. But it is now more tightly constrained than before: it encodes natural language (not constructed), written by at least two scribes with different vocabulary preferences, containing morphemes that function as grammatical markers with distinct syntactic positions. The encoding system is likely a nomenclator or similar hybrid, and the underlying language is probably (but not certainly) Romance.

The gap between "constraining the problem" and "solving the problem" remains vast. The codebook, if one existed, is almost certainly lost. Without it, or without a bilingual parallel text, full decipherment may be impossible. What computational analysis CAN do -- and what this project has done -- is eliminate wrong answers, identify structural patterns, and narrow the search space for future work.

That is a modest contribution. But in Voynich studies, modesty is a virtue that has been in desperately short supply.
