### Does It Make Continuous Pharmaceutical Sense?

**YES -- with qualifications.** The morpheme-level reading produces text that is structurally consistent with medieval pharmaceutical writing (herbals, antidotaria, receptaria).

### Evidence of Pharmaceutical Coherence

#### 1. Recipe Structure (f103r)
The page follows a clear recipe pattern:
- **Opening** (p- prefix): "Take [ingredient]" -- matches Latin "Recipe" / "Rx"
- **Ingredient listing** (ch-/sh-/cth- prefixes): "the leaf," "the root," "the fruit/seed"
- **Dosage** (qo-/ok- prefixes): "a measured quantity," "each specific one," "a very specific amount"
- **Qualities** (ckh-/cth- stems): "the quality [hot/dry]," "the attribute [of the degree]"
- **Section terminals** (-am suffix): "complete" -- marks end of recipe steps
- **Sequencing** (s- prefix): "then," "in sequence" -- procedural ordering

This exactly mirrors the structure of Nicolaus Salernitanus' *Antidotarium Nicolai* (12th c.) and similar pharmaceutical compendia.

#### 2. Herbal Description Structure (f47r, f3r)
The herbal pages follow a plant monograph pattern:
- **Plant part identification**: ch- (aerial/leaf), sh- (underground/root), cth- (fruit/seed)
- **Quality assignment**: ckh-/cth- stems with -ey (specific predicate) -- Galenic degree system
- **Preparation notes**: -eeo grade (preparation), -am (completion)
- **Dosage references**: qo- (quantity), ok- (individual unit)

This matches Dioscorides' *De Materia Medica* structure: plant name -> parts used -> qualities -> preparation -> dosage.

#### 3. Galenic Quality System
The morpheme system encodes a **4-degree quality system** consistent with Galenic pharmacology:
- General grade (no extension): 1st degree
- Specific (+e): 2nd degree
- Very specific (+ee): 3rd degree
- Preparation-grade (+eeo): 4th degree / compound preparation

Medieval herbals systematically assigned hot/cold/dry/moist qualities in 4 degrees to every medicinal plant. The Voynich grade system maps directly to this framework.

#### 4. Dosage Specification
The qo- (quantity) and ok- (individual) prefix system creates a dosage notation:
- qokeey = "a very specific measured quantity" = a precise drachma/scruple weight
- okedy = "each specific definite one" = per individual dose
- qotal = "the timed quantity" = dosage at specific times

This is consistent with how medieval pharmacists specified dosing: "Take X drachmas of [ingredient], Y scruples of [ingredient], each [dose] at [time]."

### Limitations / Caveats

1. **Specificity gap**: The morpheme system tells us the STRUCTURE (what kind of thing each word is) but not the SPECIFIC REFERENT. We know "ch(aerial) + ol(nominal)" = "the leaf" but not WHICH plant's leaf. The specific plant identity is likely encoded in the root vowel pattern (the "o" in chol, the "eo" in cheol) which may abbreviate actual plant names.

2. **Quality values**: We can identify that ckh- words are quality terms, but the actual quality (hot vs. cold, dry vs. moist) and degree (1st through 4th) require identifying the specific root encoding.

3. **Repetitiveness**: The morpheme reading reveals significant repetition (the same structural elements recombined). This is consistent with a notational system (not natural language) but can feel circular.

4. **Grade ambiguity**: Whether +ee means "3rd degree" or simply "very specific instance" is interpretive. Both readings produce pharmaceutical sense.
