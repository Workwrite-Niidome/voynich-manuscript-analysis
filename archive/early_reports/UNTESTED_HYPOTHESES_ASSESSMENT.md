## HYPOTHESIS 1: STEGANOGRAPHY (Text is meaningless filler; real message in illustrations/spacing)

### Compatibility with our statistics: INCOMPATIBLE (FATAL)

The steganography hypothesis states the text is meaningless decoy. This directly contradicts:
- **C1**: Hurst 0.78-0.99 proves long-range persistence. Random filler would give Hurst ~0.5.
- **C2**: Inter-line MI of 0.40 bits means lines are correlated with each other. Filler text would show MI ~0.
- **C3**: Prefix-suffix MI at Z=297 means internal word structure is non-random. Filler would not produce systematic morphology.
- **C7**: Zipf correlation r=0.9919 with a natural language. Random filler matching Zipf is possible (many distributions do), but matching a SPECIFIC language's frequency curve at r>0.99 is not.

### Proposed in literature: YES, partially.
Gordon Rugg (2004) proposed the text was generated using a Cardan grille over a table of syllables -- essentially a sophisticated form of meaningless-text generation designed to LOOK meaningful. Our agents already tested and rejected this (Hurst incompatible, multifractal spectrum too wide). Andreas Schinner (2007) proposed the text was generated by a stochastic process, but his analysis was challenged by Amancio et al. (2013) who showed Voynich text clusters with natural languages, not random processes.

Pure steganography where the text is truly random filler is DEAD. However, a HYBRID model where the text is meaningful AND the illustrations contain an additional layer of information is not excluded by our data. We simply have no way to test the illustration-layer claim without physical/spectral analysis of the manuscript.

### What it could explain that others cannot: Nothing. Every property of the text is already better explained by the nomenclator model.

### ONE TEST: Compute the transfer entropy from text content to illustration features (plant type, root shape, leaf count) across pages. If text is filler, transfer entropy should be zero. If text describes the illustrations, transfer entropy should be significantly positive.

### VERDICT: **ELIMINATED** as pure steganography. Hybrid steganography (text meaningful + hidden layer in illustrations) remains untestable without physical access.

## HYPOTHESIS 2: MUSICAL NOTATION (Words encode melodies or musical phrases)

### Compatibility with our statistics: PARTIALLY COMPATIBLE

Musical notation would explain:
- **C9**: Low entropy (musical phrases have constrained note transitions, like language)
- **C6**: The prefix-suffix structure could represent key signatures (prefix) + melodic patterns (root) + ornamentation (suffix)
- **C4**: Two-component structure could be melody (slow-varying) + rhythm (fast-varying)

However, it struggles with:
- **C7**: Why would musical notation match Italian word frequencies at r=0.9919? Musical phrase frequencies follow very different distributions.
- **C3**: Musical notation does not typically have agglutinative morphology with productive prefix/suffix systems
- **C5**: Two scribes writing music would use the same notation for the same piece. The A/B vocabulary divergence (Jaccard 0.172) is unexplainable -- two transcriptions of the same musical tradition would share far more vocabulary.
- **The illustrations**: The manuscript contains botanical illustrations, bathing scenes, and astronomical diagrams. Musical manuscripts of this era look completely different.

### Proposed in literature: Not seriously.
There is no peer-reviewed proposal for musical encoding. Some amateur discussions exist online but none have produced testable predictions.

### What it could explain: The low entropy and positional constraints. But these are already better explained by natural language with nomenclator encoding.

### ONE TEST: Extract the numeric sequence of characters (mapping each EVA character to a pitch value) and compute the autocorrelation function. Musical melodies show characteristic autocorrelation with peaks at intervals of 4, 8, 16 (musical phrase lengths). Natural language shows monotonic decay. If the autocorrelation shows periodic peaks, the musical hypothesis gains support.

### VERDICT: **IMPLAUSIBLE.** The Zipf-Italian correlation and illustration content are fatal. The statistical profile matches language, not music.

## HYPOTHESIS 3: NUMERICAL/COMPUTATIONAL (Text encodes numbers -- astronomical tables, calculations)

### Compatibility with our statistics: PARTIALLY COMPATIBLE

Number encoding could explain:
- **C9**: Numerical tables have low entropy (digits are constrained)
- **C4**: Two-component structure could be integer part + fractional part, or degree + minute

However, it fails on:
- **C1/C2**: Astronomical tables typically show LOW Hurst exponents because sequential entries are independent observations. The Voynich's Hurst of 0.78-0.99 indicates strong persistence -- each "word" is heavily influenced by preceding words. This is characteristic of language, not tabular data.
- **C6**: Productive morphology (ch-/sh- prefixes) makes no sense for number representation. Numbers don't have grammatical prefixes.
- **C7**: Number sequences do not follow Zipf's law with r=0.9919 correlation to Italian word frequencies.
- **C3**: Numbers do not have prefix-suffix mutual information at Z=297.

### Proposed in literature: YES, partially.
Jorge Stolfi proposed that the text might encode a numerical system, noting that Voynichese words have a very regular structure that could represent place-value numbers. However, his own analysis showed the word-internal structure is more complex than simple number encoding. The astronomical section (with circular diagrams) has been proposed to contain astronomical data, but the text in those sections has the SAME statistical properties as the herbal sections.

### What it could explain: The regularity of word structure. But morphological productivity contradicts number encoding.

### ONE TEST: If words encode numbers, then the CHARACTER at each position within a word should be independent of characters at other positions (like digits in a number). Compute the MI between character positions 1-2, 2-3, 3-4, etc. within words. For numbers: MI should be near zero. For language: MI should be positive. Our existing prefix-suffix MI of 0.075 (Z=297) already suggests language, but a FULL position-by-position MI matrix would be definitive.

### VERDICT: **EFFECTIVELY ELIMINATED** by prefix-suffix MI and Hurst exponent. The text behaves like language, not numbers.

## HYPOTHESIS 4: MIXED CONTENT (Some pages meaningful, others filler)

### Compatibility with our statistics: COMPATIBLE (Cannot be excluded)

This hypothesis is subtle. If SOME pages are real and others are padding:
- **C1-C3**: The meaningful pages would produce the strong statistical signals we observe. If filler pages were generated by a sophisticated method (like copying from the meaningful pages with substitutions), the aggregate statistics might still look linguistic.
- **C5**: The A/B split could ITSELF be the meaningful/filler division. One scribe writes real content; the other fills pages.
- **C4**: The two-component entropy decay could literally be two types of pages (meaningful component + filler component), not two components within pages.

### Proposed in literature: YES, implicitly.
Prescott Currier's A/B language distinction (1976) was originally proposed as two different "languages" or encoding systems. Some researchers have suggested that one might be a draft/practice version. The "mixed content" hypothesis is a natural extension of this, though nobody has explicitly proposed that entire sections are meaningless filler.

### What it could explain:
- The extreme vocabulary divergence between A and B (Jaccard 0.172) -- if B sections are systematically different in PURPOSE from A
- The fact that certain sections (biological, cosmological) seem harder to interpret than others
- The recipe section's anomalous statistics (low entropy + high Lyapunov = "deterministic chaos" -- could be formulaic filler)

### HOWEVER: Our own Currier A/B analysis ALREADY found that BOTH A and B contain meaningful linguistic structure. Both have inter-line MI, both follow Zipf's law, both have productive morphology. If one were filler, its statistics would be degraded. They are not.

### ONE TEST: Compute the cross-entropy between A-pages and B-pages. If both are meaningful text in related systems, cross-entropy should be moderate (one can partially predict the other). If one is filler, cross-entropy should be very high (one cannot predict the other at all). Compare: H(A|B) vs H(B|A). Asymmetry would reveal which is more "predictable" and which is more "surprising."

### VERDICT: **WEAKLY POSSIBLE but unlikely.** Both A and B show independent linguistic structure. The most likely explanation remains two scribes with different conventions, not real vs. filler content.

## HYPOTHESIS 5: VISUAL ENCODING (Meaning is in glyph SHAPES, not sequences)

### Compatibility with our statistics: INCOMPATIBLE (FATAL)

If meaning were in glyph shapes (pictographic elements), then:
- **C1/C2**: Sequential statistics (Hurst, MI between lines) would be irrelevant or zero, because the sequence doesn't carry meaning. But we MEASURED strong sequential structure. This is fatal.
- **C3**: Prefix-suffix MI within words requires that the sequence matters. If each glyph is an independent pictographic element, there should be no MI between prefix and suffix positions.
- **C6**: Productive morphology requires that the ORDER of elements matters. Pictographic systems do not have productive morphological rules.
- **C7**: Zipf's law in a pictographic system would reflect concept frequency, not language frequency. The r=0.9919 match to Italian is unexplainable.

### Proposed in literature: Not seriously.
Some fringe proposals exist suggesting the glyphs are modified forms of alchemical symbols or pictograms, but none have produced systematic analyses.

### What it could explain: Nothing that isn't better explained by existing hypotheses.

### ONE TEST: If meaning is in shape, then ANAGRAMMING words (scrambling character order within each word) should preserve meaning and therefore preserve statistical properties. Scramble all words internally and recompute Hurst, MI, and entropy. For sequential encoding: statistics should degrade dramatically. For visual/pictographic: statistics should be preserved.

### VERDICT: **ELIMINATED.** Sequential structure is proven.

## HYPOTHESIS 6: PARTIAL ENCODING (Only first word of each paragraph is meaningful)

### Compatibility with our statistics: INCOMPATIBLE

If only the first word of each paragraph carries meaning:
- **C2**: Inter-line MI of 0.40 bits means EVERY line is correlated with adjacent lines. If only paragraph-initial words mattered, the MI between non-initial lines should be zero. It isn't.
- **C1**: Hurst exponent computed across the ENTIRE text shows persistence. If most text were formulaic padding, the Hurst of the non-initial-word portions should be lower. We measured consistently high Hurst across all sections.
- **C10**: The ii null character appears in both line-initial and line-internal positions. If only first words mattered, null characters in non-first positions would be pointless.

### Proposed in literature: A variant was proposed.
Leo Levitov (1987) proposed that the text was a form of "verbose" encoding where meaning was spread thinly across many words. The "partial encoding" hypothesis is the opposite extreme -- meaning concentrated in a few words. Neither has gained traction. Torsten Timm (2014) proposed that later lines on each page were generated by copying from earlier lines with modifications, which is a softer version of "first words real, rest derived."

### What it could explain: The high proportion of apparent function words (if everything after the first word is filler with a formulaic structure).

### ONE TEST: Extract only the first word of each paragraph/section. Compute entropy and Zipf distribution of this subset alone. If these are meaningful labels, they should have HIGHER entropy than the bulk text (more information per word) and a different Zipf exponent. Compare with the entropy of non-initial words. A significant difference would support partial encoding.

### VERDICT: **ELIMINATED** by inter-line MI. The correlation structure extends across entire lines, not just first words.

## HYPOTHESIS 7: MULTIPLE LAYERS (Two simultaneous meanings -- character-level AND word-level)

### Compatibility with our statistics: COMPATIBLE (Intriguing)

This is actually CONSISTENT with our findings:
- **C4**: The two-component entropy decay (7.7x ratio) could literally be two layers of meaning -- one decaying quickly (character-level cipher) and one decaying slowly (word-level code). This is EXACTLY what a nomenclator produces. Our own conclusion IS a two-layer system.
- **C3**: Prefix-suffix MI could reflect constraints at BOTH levels simultaneously.
- **C6**: The morphological structure could serve double duty -- prefixes carry category information (one layer) while suffixes carry grammatical information (another layer).

### Proposed in literature: YES -- this IS essentially the nomenclator hypothesis.
Our own agents arrived at this independently. The nomenclator model (code + cipher hybrid) is precisely a multi-layer system: cipher words carry meaning at the character level (substitution cipher of function words), while code words carry meaning at the word level (arbitrary codebook assignments). The "multiple layers" hypothesis, properly formulated, IS our leading hypothesis.

### What it could explain: Everything. This is why it's our top model.

### ONE TEST: Already partially done. The conditional entropy decay showing two distinct components IS the test. To strengthen: compute the entropy decay profile for CIPHER words (function words) and CODE words (content words) separately. If there are truly two layers, the cipher words should show steep entropy decay (character-level structure) and the code words should show flat entropy decay (word-level meaning, character sequence arbitrary).

### VERDICT: **ALREADY OUR LEADING MODEL** (the nomenclator). Not truly "untested" -- we tested it and it's the winner. However, the specific possibility that BOTH layers carry INDEPENDENT meaning (not just code+cipher but e.g. one reading for characters, a different reading for word sequences) is a refinement we haven't explored.

## HYPOTHESIS 8: PHONETIC BUT NON-EUROPEAN (Basque, Georgian, extinct language)

### Compatibility with our statistics: PARTIALLY COMPATIBLE

**Basque**:
- Basque is agglutinative, which matches C6 (productive morphology).
- Basque has a small phoneme inventory (5 vowels, ~20 consonants), consistent with the ~20 EVA characters.
- Basque word frequencies would NOT match Italian at r=0.9919 (C7). This is a problem.
- Basque has ergative-absolutive grammar, which would produce DIFFERENT word-order statistics than the Romance-like patterns we observe.
- Basque was spoken in a region with no known connection to the manuscript's provenance (Northern Italy).

**Georgian**:
- Georgian has a large alphabet (38 characters), much larger than EVA's ~20. Mismatch.
- Georgian is agglutinative and would produce long words consistent with Voynich. But the specific prefix-suffix patterns don't match Georgian morphology.
- Geographic connection: extremely weak. No Georgian community in 15th-century Northern Italy.

**Extinct language** (e.g., Cuman-Kipchak, Dalmatian, Mozarabic):
- Our agents ALREADY tested Cuman-Kipchak (Turkic) via vowel harmony. It failed (58% = chance).
- **Dalmatian** (extinct Romance language of the Adriatic coast): This is actually interesting. Dalmatian was a Romance language spoken in coastal Croatia/Montenegro until 1898. A 15th-century Dalmatian text would be Romance (matching C7) but sufficiently distinct from Italian/Occitan that our specific phonetic mappings would fail. This has NOT been tested.
- **Mozarabic** (extinct Romance language of Iberian Muslims): Another Romance language that could match our statistics while failing specific Italian decoding. Also NOT tested.

### Proposed in literature: YES for some.
- Basque was proposed by James Finn (no peer review).
- Georgian has not been seriously proposed.
- Stephen Bax (2014) proposed a mixed approach identifying some words as matching various languages including Arabic-influenced medieval vocabulary.
- Dalmatian and Mozarabic have NOT been proposed, but they are compelling because they are EXTINCT Romance languages that would match our "Romance but not quite Italian" statistical profile.

### What it could explain:
The critical puzzle that our nomenclator hypothesis cannot resolve: WHY specific Italian/Occitan decoding attempts fail while the statistical profile matches Romance. An extinct or marginal Romance language would produce exactly this pattern -- statistically Romance, but no specific modern language fits.

### ONE TEST:
For **Dalmatian**: Dalmatian preserved Latin consonant clusters that Italian simplified (e.g., Latin "factum" -> Italian "fatto" but Dalmatian "fuat"). Check whether the EVA character sequences contain consonant cluster patterns that match Dalmatian's known phonological rules rather than Italian simplification rules. Specifically: does the ct- cluster in "cthol", "cthor", "cthy" map to preserved Latin clusters that Italian would have simplified?

### VERDICT: **BASQUE and GEORGIAN eliminated** (no geographic/statistical match). **DALMATIAN and MOZARABIC are genuinely untested and plausible.** This is a real gap in our analysis.

## HYPOTHESIS 9: ENCRYPTED ARAMAIC/SYRIAC (Middle Eastern Christian medical text)

### Compatibility with our statistics: PARTIALLY COMPATIBLE

Syriac is a Semitic language with:
- An abjad writing system (consonants primary, vowels secondary) -- this could explain the low entropy (C9) if EVA consonant characters carry more information than EVA vowel characters
- Right-to-left writing -- the manuscript may need to be read right-to-left (some researchers have proposed this)
- Rich morphological system (root + pattern) -- Semitic root-and-pattern morphology is DIFFERENT from the agglutinative prefix-suffix system we observe (C6). Semitic morphology involves vowel infixation within triconsonantal roots, while Voynich shows linear prefix-root-suffix concatenation. This is a MISMATCH.
- Syriac medical texts existed and circulated in medieval Italy via Arabic intermediaries

**Critical problem**:
- C7: Syriac word frequencies would NOT match Italian at r=0.9919. Semitic language frequency distributions are structurally different from Romance.
- C6: Semitic root-and-pattern morphology (k-t-b -> katab, kutib, kitab) looks nothing like the linear ch-ol, ch-edy, sh-ol, sh-edy pattern we observe. The Voynich pattern is LINEAR CONCATENATION, not infixation.
- C3: The prefix-suffix MI pattern in Voynich (Z=297) is characteristic of agglutinative or fusional languages, not Semitic templatic morphology.

### Proposed in literature: YES, partially.
The Hebrew-script hypothesis has been proposed by several researchers (including Tim Carter Clausen recently). Our agents tested Hebrew-based cipher and found visual similarity (6.8/10) but decoding failure. Syriac specifically has not been the focus, but the statistical arguments against it are the same as against Hebrew -- the morphological pattern is wrong for Semitic.

### What it could explain: The Hebrew-like script appearance (Syriac and Hebrew scripts are related).

### ONE TEST: Semitic languages have a distinctive property: triconsonantal roots. If the text is Syriac, we should be able to identify recurring three-consonant patterns that appear with different vowel patterns between them (like Arabic k-t-b appearing as katab, kutub, kitab). Extract all consonant-only skeletons from EVA words and test whether triconsonantal patterns recur with different vowel fills. For Semitic: strong signal. For Romance/agglutinative: no signal.

### VERDICT: **EFFECTIVELY ELIMINATED.** The morphological structure is linear agglutinative (prefix + root + suffix), not Semitic templatic (consonantal root + vowel pattern). The r=0.9919 match to Italian frequencies is also incompatible with a Semitic source language.

## HYPOTHESIS 10: TIME-SHIFTED ITALIAN (13th-century or 16th-century Italian)

### Compatibility with our statistics: FULLY COMPATIBLE

This is highly plausible because:
- **C7**: Word frequency distributions are stable within a language family across centuries. r=0.9919 with modern Italian would also hold for 13th-16th century Italian.
- **C6**: Italian morphology has been relatively stable since the 13th century. Prefix/suffix systems would be similar.
- **C1-C4**: All statistical properties would be preserved because it's still Italian.
- **C5**: Two scribes writing in the same era with dialect variation -- unchanged.

**13th-century Italian** (Duecento):
- More Latin influence, less standardized spelling
- Stronger dialectal variation (Sicilian School vs. Tuscan vs. Northern)
- More preserved Latin consonant clusters
- Would explain why modern Italian decoding attempts fail on specifics

**16th-century Italian** (Cinquecento):
- More standardized but still with regional variation
- Problematic because radiocarbon dating places the vellum at 1404-1438
- Text could be later than vellum (vellum can be reused), but this adds complexity without explanatory gain

### Proposed in literature: Implicitly.
Most researchers assume 15th-century Italian. The specific proposal that the language might be OLDER than the vellum (13th-century Italian preserved by copying onto newer vellum) has not been systematically explored. The Carrara Herbal (c.1400) translates Arabic medical text into PADUAN DIALECT of the period, which differs significantly from standard Tuscan Italian. Our agents identified the Carrara Herbal as the closest parallel but did not test whether Paduan dialect specifically explains the decoding failures.

### What it could explain:
- Why generic Italian statistical tests pass but specific word decodings fail
- Why "dalla" frequency doesn't match (it might match an earlier/different register of Italian)
- Why Occitan scores equally well (13th-century Northern Italian dialects were closer to Occitan than modern Italian is)

### ONE TEST: Obtain a digital text of the Carrara Herbal (Paduan dialect, c.1400) or another Northern Italian medical text from the 13th-14th century. Compute word frequency distribution and compare with Voynich cipher-word frequencies. If the correlation IMPROVES over the r=0.9919 we got with modern Italian, the time-shifted hypothesis gains strong support. Specifically, check whether the function-word frequencies (di, e, il, la, del, per, con) match better in 14th-century Paduan than in modern standard Italian.

### VERDICT: **VIABLE AND UNDERTESTED.** This is less a separate hypothesis than a REFINEMENT of our existing Italian nomenclator model. The specific dialect and century matter for decoding, and we have not controlled for this.

## THE HYPOTHESIS WE MISSED: ABBREVIATION SYSTEM / NOTAE (Hypothesis 11)

After reviewing all 10 proposed hypotheses and our existing analysis, there is one major hypothesis that was MENTIONED but never rigorously tested:

### HYPOTHESIS 11: MEDIEVAL PHARMACEUTICAL SHORTHAND (Notae / Abbreviation System)

**The claim**: The Voynich text is not a cipher at all. It is a SHORTHAND NOTATION SYSTEM -- like medieval "notae" (abbreviation marks) used by physicians and apothecaries to write prescriptions quickly. Each "word" is a cluster of abbreviation symbols, not a cipher encoding of a natural language word.

**Why this matters**:
- Medieval pharmaceutical shorthand DID exist. Apothecaries used standard abbreviation systems (ana = equal parts, Rx = recipe/take, q.s. = quantum sufficit, etc.)
- These systems look alien to modern readers
- They have their OWN statistical properties distinct from both natural language and cipher
- They are NOT ciphers (no key needed) but ARE opaque without knowledge of the system

**Compatibility with our statistics**:
- **C1/C2**: Shorthand text has long-range correlations (prescriptions follow formulaic structures)
- **C4**: Two-component structure = structural markers (take, mix, apply) + ingredient abbreviations
- **C6**: Productive morphology = abbreviation rules (prefix marks category, suffix marks quantity/preparation)
- **C7**: Function-word frequencies would match the source language (Italian/Latin medical register)
- **C9**: Low entropy because abbreviation systems are DESIGNED to be compact and regular

**Why it wasn't properly tested**: Our agents MENTIONED "pharmaceutical shorthand" as the top consensus hypothesis in the Grand Synthesis but then PIVOTED to the nomenclator model and never actually tested the shorthand hypothesis against specific properties of known medieval abbreviation systems.

**The critical difference from nomenclator**: A nomenclator uses ARBITRARY code assignments (you need a codebook). An abbreviation system uses SYSTEMATIC rules (you need to know the rules, but there IS no codebook -- the abbreviations are logical once you know the system). If the Voynich is shorthand rather than nomenclator, it IS fully decodable without finding a lost codebook.

**ONE TEST**: Medieval pharmaceutical abbreviation systems have a specific property: the same ROOT with different DIACRITICAL MARKS means different things. In the EVA transcription, the ch/sh distinction could represent two different diacritical marks on the SAME base character. If so, "chol" and "shol" would be the SAME root concept with different modification marks (like "R" with a line through it vs "R" with a dot above it). Test: do ch-words and sh-words appear in COMPLEMENTARY DISTRIBUTION (never on the same page) or CONTRASTIVE DISTRIBUTION (same page, different positions)? Our data shows they DO appear on the same pages but in different proportions per scribe. This is more consistent with nomenclator than shorthand, but the test hasn't been done formally.

## THE REAL GAP IN OUR ANALYSIS

After assessing all 10 hypotheses plus the shorthand hypothesis I identified, the GENUINE gap is this:

**We tested "Italian" as a monolithic concept. We never tested SPECIFIC HISTORICAL DIALECTS of Northern Italian Romance.**

The three highest-priority untested hypotheses (H10, H8b, H11) all point to the same blind spot: we know the text is statistically Romance, but we treated "Romance" as if it meant "modern standard Italian." The actual language could be:

1. **14th-century Paduan dialect** (as used in the Carrara Herbal) -- a language that is superficially similar to Italian but has significant vocabulary, spelling, and grammatical differences
2. **Dalmatian** -- an extinct Romance language that shares many features with Northern Italian but preserves Latin features that Italian lost
3. **A medical register/jargon** that differs from standard literary Italian in the same way modern medical English differs from conversational English

The ONE test that addresses all three simultaneously:

**Obtain digitized texts from 14th-15th century Northern Italian medical manuscripts (Carrara Herbal, Tacuinum Sanitatis, Serapion translations) and compute word frequency distributions, bigram statistics, and function word ratios. Compare these with the Voynich cipher-word statistics. If the correlation exceeds r=0.9919, we have identified the specific register and dialect. If it doesn't, the text is more exotic than any known Northern Italian medical text.**

This is the single most productive next step that our 72+ agents never took.
