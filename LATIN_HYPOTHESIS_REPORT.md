Italian words almost universally end in vowels (-a, -e, -i, -o). The exceptions are a handful of function words (in, con, per, del, il, un) and foreign loans. An Italian text with 58% consonant-final words is structurally impossible.

Latin, by contrast, has abundant consonant-final words:
- Nouns: radix, semen, flores, cortex, virtus, natura(m)
- Verbs: est, habet, valet, fecit
- Function words: et, in, ad, cum, sed
- Adjectives: calid-am/-um, sicc-am/-um (accusative forms)

**This single metric eliminates Italian and is consistent with Latin.**

## 4. KEY REINTERPRETATIONS (Italian → Latin)

### chol: "foglia" (leaf) → "calida" (hot)

The previous Italian mapping assigned chol = foglia (leaf). Under the Latin hypothesis, chol = calida (hot, humoral quality). This is a better fit because:

1. **Every** Latin herbal entry classifies the plant by four humoral qualities: calida/frigida/sicca/humida
2. "calida" appears in virtually every herbal description: "herba X est calida in primo gradu et sicca in secundo"
3. The ch-/sh- prefix opposition may encode calida/frigida (hot/cold) — paired opposites
4. Frequency matches: calida ~1-2% in Latin herbals vs chol ~1% in Voynich

### daiin: "di" (of) → "et" (and)

The previous Italian mapping: daiin = di (of). Under Latin: daiin = et (and).

- "et" is the single most frequent word in Latin prose
- The herbal/recipe frequency ratio (3.71%/1.10% = 3.4x) matches "et": it links descriptions in herbals ("est calida **et** sicca") but is less needed in recipes where imperatives dominate ("Recipe... tere... misce...")
- Italian "di" would NOT show this herbal>recipe skew because "di" is equally frequent in both contexts ("olio **di** camomilla", "radice **di** costo")

### ol: "la/il" (the) → "est" (is)

Latin has NO articles. The previous mapping of ol = la/il is impossible if the language is Latin. Instead, ol = est (is/it is) fits:
- "herba est calida" (the herb is hot)
- High frequency copula
- Herbal section dominant (descriptions use "est" constantly)

## 6. DOES THE LATIN READING MAKE MORE PHARMACEUTICAL SENSE?

### YES, and here is why:

#### A. The humoral system is LATIN

Medieval pharmaceutical texts classify every plant by four qualities on a four-degree scale:
```
"Herba X est calida in primo gradu et sicca in secundo gradu."
(Herb X is hot in the first degree and dry in the second degree.)
```

This formula appears on virtually EVERY page of a Latin herbal. The ch-/sh- prefix opposition mapping to calida/frigida (hot/cold) and the cth- prefix mapping to sicca (dry) creates a coherent humoral classification system that would explain why these words appear on every plant page.

Italian herbals translated this system but often used vernacular paraphrases ("questa erba e calda nel primo grado" — much longer). The Voynich's compact word forms match Latin's economy better.

#### B. Latin was THE pharmaceutical language

In the 15th century:
- **Circa Instans** (the standard herbal reference) — Latin
- **Antidotarium Nicolai** (the standard recipe book) — Latin
- **Macer Floridus** (herbal poem) — Latin
- University pharmacy curricula — Latin
- Apothecary licensing exams — Latin

Even Italian pharmacists who spoke vernacular Italian wrote their professional texts in Latin. A pharmaceutical manuscript from this period is *expected* to be in Latin.

#### C. The "ana" mapping (okaiin = ana)

"ana" is the single most characteristic word of pharmaceutical Latin. It means "each in equal parts" and appears in virtually every recipe:
```
"Recipe radicis et foliorum ana drachmam unam"
(Take root and leaves, each one dram)
```

The previous Italian mapping had okaiin = ogni (each) which is similar but less specifically pharmaceutical. The Latin "ana" is a technical term with no Italian equivalent; Italian recipes borrowed it directly from Latin.

#### D. Verb-final word order (SOV)

Latin's default word order is Subject-Object-Verb (SOV):
```
"Haec herba virtutem magnam habet."
(This herb great-virtue has.)
```

The verb-final tendency detected in Voynich line templates matches Latin SOV but conflicts with Italian SVO:
```
Italian: "Questa erba ha una grande virtu." (SVO)
Latin:   "Haec herba virtutem magnam habet." (SOV)
```

#### E. No preposition+article contractions

Italian has obligatory contractions: di+il=del, di+la=della, a+il=al, in+il=nel, etc. These contracted forms (del, della, dello, nell', all', etc.) would be among the most frequent words in any Italian text. Their complete absence in Voynich is natural in Latin (which has no articles) but anomalous in Italian.

The structural similarities between Voynich and Romance languages are explained by Voynich encoding their parent language.
