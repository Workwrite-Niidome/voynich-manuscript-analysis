# Venetian Diplomatic Ciphers & Pharmaceutical Secrecy in 15th-Century Northern Italy

## Web Research Findings (2026-04-10)

## 1. Venetian Council of Ten Ciphers (1400-1450)

### Earliest Venetian Ciphers

- **1411**: The earliest evidence for enciphered diplomatic correspondence in Venice. This is the oldest known example of Venetian ciphers preserved in the Archivio di Stato di Venezia (ASVE).
- **1430s**: Merchant Andrea Barbigo used his own cipher in confidential communications with his agent in the Levant, showing that cipher use extended beyond diplomacy into commerce.
- Venice was NOT the first Italian state to use ciphers. The **Duchy of Mantua** (1401) and **Florence** (1424) have earlier surviving examples.

### Were They Nomenclators?

**Yes, from the very beginning.** Italian diplomatic ciphers of this era were nomenclators -- hybrid systems combining:
1. A **substitution cipher alphabet** (usually with homophones for vowels)
2. A **small codebook** (nomenclator) encoding common words, names, and places
3. **Nulls** (meaningless symbols inserted to confuse analysis)
4. **Syllable tables** (arbitrary symbols for common syllable pairs)

The 1401 Mantuan cipher already had all these features:
- Reversed-alphabet substitution (A->Z, B->Y, etc.)
- Four different shapes per vowel (homophonic substitution)
- A nomenclator section with code symbols for common words

By the 1450s, the Milanese cipher (Tranchedini) had grown to **253 signs**: 55 for letters, 12 for double letters, 8 for nulls, 65 for syllables, 113 for words.

### Preserved Codebooks in the Archivio di Stato

**Yes, original cipher keys are preserved.** Key archival resources:

- **ASVE (Archivio di Stato di Venezia)**: Contains cipher keys, encrypted diplomatic correspondence, and the Amadi manuscript (700 pages, acquired 1588)
- **Luigi Pasini** (1835-1885), archivist at the ASVE, studied and reorganized the cipher keys in the archive. He classified each cipher by the sign used to represent the letter "A". His 1872 booklet "Delle scritture in cifra usate nella Republica di Venezia" documents the collection.
- **Agostino Amadi's "Delle Ziffre"** (before 1588): A 700-page, 10-volume cryptology manual presenting cipher methods in multiple languages (Greek, Arabic, Latin, Italian). Volume 1 alone discusses 80+ ciphering methods with diagrams, tables, cipher wheels, and scrolls. Acquired by the Council of Ten from Amadi's widow.
- **Mantua State Archives**: 55 cipher keys preserved for the years 1401-1416 related to Gonzaga diplomatic correspondence.
- **Milan State Archives**: Tranchedini cipher key and nomenclator dated 14 March 1448.

### The Professionalization of Venetian Cryptography

- **1506**: Giovanni Soro hired as Venice's first professional cipher breaker (cipher secretary) by the Council of Ten
- Soro could decipher the codes of most European courts; even the Papal Curia hired him
- By 1542, Soro had two assistants and a secret office in the Doge's Palace above the Sala di Segret
- Before his death in 1544, the Council of Ten commissioned him to train successors, establishing a bureau of professional cryptographers that lasted over a century
- Professional cifristi (cipher secretaries) had to pass entrance exams, study frequency analysis and number theory, and learn multiple languages

### Key Scholarly References

- Ioanna Iordanou, *Venice's Secret Service: Organizing Intelligence in the Renaissance* (Oxford UP, 2019)
- D.P.J.A. Scheers & Marco Ponzi, *The Venetian Ciphers of Agostino Amadi* (3 volumes)
- Luigi Pasini, *Delle scritture in cifra usate nella Republica di Venezia* (1872)
- Aloys Meister, two volumes on Italian 15th-century cryptography and papal ciphers (still frequently cited)

## 2. Venetian Pharmaceutical Secrecy

### Theriac (Theriaca) -- Venice's Most Valuable Pharmaceutical Export

**How was the recipe protected?**

The Venetian approach was a fascinating **paradox of secrecy and publicity**:

1. **Secret recipe, public production**: The Venetian speziali (pharmacists) developed "a supposedly more correct recipe for theriac, which they kept secret for foreigners." Yet the actual production was mandated to occur publicly.

2. **Public ceremony**: The Magistrato alla Sanita demanded:
   - All ingredients displayed outside the shop for **three days** before production
   - Manufacturing performed in public, in front of the shop
   - Physicians (protomedico) inspected every ingredient
   - Workers in colorful attire ground ingredients in large mortars under supervision
   - Final jars were **officially and ceremoniously sealed** under authority's watchful eyes

3. **Licensed monopoly**: Production was restricted to authorized pharmacies called *triacanti* -- approximately 40 licensed establishments (later reduced to about 4). Production was limited to one or two annual cycles.

4. **Brand authentication**: Venice designed stamps and seals for authenticated theriac, a precursor to brand logos.

5. **Monopoly broken in 1669**: French apothecary Moyse Charas published the formula, seeking to break the Venetian monopoly.

### Why Venice Dominated

Venice's position in the spice trade gave its apothecaries access to recently imported, high-quality pharmaceutical simples (raw ingredients) from the East. The theriac recipe required dozens of exotic ingredients.

### Guild Structure

- **Arti dei Medici e degli Speziali**: Joint organization of physicians and apothecaries, with statutes from 1258
- **Collegio degli Speziali**: Later independent apothecary guild with strict rules on education, membership, and medicine production
- Regulation by **Giustizia Vecchia** (early), then **Magistrato alla Sanita** (later)

### Was There Documented Use of Codes for Pharmaceutical Recipes?

**No direct evidence found** of formal cipher systems used specifically for pharmaceutical recipe secrecy. However:

- Apothecaries used **Latin abbreviations and specialized notation** (dram, scruple, ounce symbols) that functioned as a de facto specialist code
- Adriano Cappelli's *Lexicon Abbreviaturarum* documents medieval brachigraphic signs from Vulgar Latin and Italian texts, originating from Roman sigla and Tironian notes
- The theriac recipe was protected primarily through **institutional control** (licensing, public ceremony, guild membership) rather than cryptographic encoding
- The Council of Ten's intelligence operations **did** include pharmaceutical knowledge -- specifically the development of poisons and antidotes, using Jewish doctors, merchants, and alchemists with access to exotic substances

### Council of Ten and Poisons

The Republic of Venice was notorious as the "homeland of poisons." Archives show at least **34 government-sponsored assassination attempts involving poison** between 1400 and 1755. Christian professors of chemistry and botany were employed by the Council to compound ingredients. Their pharmaceutical knowledge was thus directly connected to state intelligence operations.

## 3. Giovanni Fontana's Ciphers (Padua, 1420s)

### Biographical Summary

- **Born**: c. 1395, Venice (or Padua)
- **Education**: University of Padua -- degree in arts (1418), degree in medicine (1421)
- **Career**: Municipal physician of Udine; engineer, inventor, writer
- **Died**: c. 1455

### His Cipher System

Fontana used a **mnemonic-based cipher using circles and tickmarks**:

**Vowels** -- represented by circles with directional marks:
- A, U: tickmarks facing east/west
- E, O: tickmarks facing north/south
- I: a squished circle

**Consonants** -- several construction methods:
- **Mirroring** (b, d, p, q): horizontally flipped letters with strokes and tickmarks
- **Loop-based** (s, t): closed loops or modified circles
- **Stroke-counting** (h, k, m, n): circles with attached strokes matching the letter's structure
- **Composite forms** (x, y, z, c, f, g): combinations of crosses, angles, and circles

Key characteristics:
- A **simple, rational cipher based on signs without letters or numbers**
- Prioritized **memorability** over security (mnemonic design)
- Consisted "almost entirely of straight lines and circles"
- Used in both *Bellicorum instrumentorum liber* (c. 1420s) and *Secretum de thesauro* (c. 1430)

### His Works

1. **Bellicorum instrumentorum liber** (c. 1420-1430): Siege engines, fountains, pumps, magic lanterns, rocket-propelled animals, robotic automata, alchemical furnaces. Preserved at Bayerische Staatsbibliothek, Munich.
2. **Secretum de thesauro experimentorum ymaginationis hominum** (c. 1430): About memory and mnemonic devices. Entirely in cipher except for table of contents, title, and concluding formula. Preserved at Bibliotheque nationale de France (NAL 635).

### Fontana's Associates at Padua

His academic promoters at the University of Padua included:
- **Paolo Nicoletti da Udine** ("Paul of Venice," c. 1370-1429) -- most prominent mentor, professor of logic and philosophy
- **Antonio Cermisone** -- physician, whose Consilia were published
- **Bartolomeo da Montagnana** (c. 1380-1452) -- Paduan physician, authored an Antidotarium with recipes for ointments, oils, pills, etc.
- **Galeazzo di Santa Sofia** (d. 1427) -- physician and anatomist at Padua and Bologna
- **Prosdocimo de' Beldomandi** (d. 1428) -- mathematician, music theorist, and physician
- **Stefano de' Dottori** -- academic promoter
- **Biagio da Parma** -- academic promoter

### Other Cipher Manuscripts from the Padua Circle

**No other cipher manuscripts from Fontana's immediate circle have been identified.** His cipher system appears to be unique to his personal works. However, the intellectual environment at Padua in the 1410s-1420s was rich with technical, medical, and mathematical knowledge that could have inspired notation systems.

### Fontana and the Voynich Manuscript

Some scholars note that Fontana's illustrations "slightly resemble" Voynich manuscript imagery, but:
- Fontana did NOT use the Voynich script -- he used a simple substitution cipher
- His cipher system is structurally different from Voynichese
- Statistical analysis shows the Voynich manuscript is NOT consistent with a simple substitution cipher
- The connection is circumstantial: contemporary time period, same geographic region, same university, similar technical/medical interests

## 4. University of Padua Medical Faculty (1400-1440)

### Key Professors in the Exact Period

| Name | Dates | Role |
|------|-------|------|
| Jacopo da Forli | c. 1364-1414 | Physician, philosopher, professor at Padua |
| Galeazzo di Santa Sofia | d. 1427 | Physician, anatomist (Padua and Bologna) |
| Leonardo Buffi da Bertipaglia | taught 1402-1429 | Authored *De antidotis*, anatomical works |
| Paolo Nicoletti (Paul of Venice) | c. 1370-1429 | Logic, philosophy |
| Prosdocimo de' Beldomandi | d. 1428 | Mathematics, music theory, medicine |
| Antonio Cermisone | active early 15th c. | Medical consilia |
| Bartolomeo da Montagnana | c. 1380-1452 | Medical consilia, Antidotarium |

### Pharmacy and Botany Teaching

- **Leonardo Buffi** wrote *De antidotis* (on antidotes/pharmaceutical preparations) and *De aquis conficiendis ad pellendas aegritudines* (on preparing healing waters)
- **Bartolomeo da Montagnana** authored an **Antidotarium** containing recipes for ointments, emplastra (patches), oils, powders, pills, and pessaries -- this is essentially a pharmaceutical recipe book
- Dissection was well-established by the early 1400s at Padua
- Dedicated botany chairs were established later (the famous botanical garden was founded in 1545)

### Student Notebooks

- A notable surviving **student notebook** (XXIII F 54) is from Padua, but dates to 1551-1552 (Georg Handsch). No 15th-century Padua student notebooks were identified in the search results.
- Medical manuscripts from this period used extensive **scribal abbreviation systems** inherited from Latin tradition, including Tironian notes and specialized medical/pharmaceutical symbols

### Known Code/Notation Systems in Teaching

No formal cipher or code systems were identified as being used in medical teaching. However:
- The **apothecary measurement system** (special symbols for dram, scruple, ounce, etc.) functioned as specialized notation
- Cappelli's *Lexicon Abbreviaturarum* catalogs medieval abbreviation systems from this milieu
- Tironian notes (Roman shorthand) were still in use and known to scholars

## 5. Leon Battista Alberti (1404-1472)

### Early Life -- The Venice/Padua Connection

- **Born**: February 14, 1404, Genoa (Florentine family in exile)
- Father Lorenzo moved the family to **Venice** soon after birth
- **c. 1414-1418**: Attended the school of **Gasparino Barzizza** in **Padua**
  - Barzizza (c. 1360-1431) was a humanist who taught at Padua 1407-1421
  - He also taught **Vittorino da Feltre** (who later founded the famous school in Mantua)
- Later studied law at the **University of Bologna** (degree 1428)

### Cipher Contributions

- **1467**: Wrote *De componendis cifris* ("On Composing Ciphers"), describing the first polyalphabetic cipher
- Invented the **cipher disk** (Formula): two concentric disks with alphabets, the inner one (Mobilis) rotating against the outer (Stabilis)
- The idea arose from a conversation with papal secretary **Leonardo Dati** about movable type printing

### Connection to Venice/Padua Pharmaceutical Circles

**No direct pharmaceutical connection found.** Alberti was primarily an architect, artist, mathematician, and humanist. His cipher work was motivated by diplomatic/papal communication needs, not pharmaceutical secrecy.

However, his **formative years in Venice and Padua** (c. 1404-1418) overlap exactly with:
- The earliest Venetian cipher use (1411)
- Fontana's education at Padua (1414-1421)
- The active period of Bartolomeo da Montagnana and other medical faculty

**Alberti and Fontana were at the University of Padua at approximately the same time** (c. 1414-1418), both under teachers connected to the same intellectual circle. Whether they knew each other is undocumented.

## 6. Cicco Simonetta (Milan, 1410-1480)

### His Treatise

- **1474**: Wrote *Regulae ad extrahendum litteras ziferatas sine exemplo* ("Rules for extracting enciphered letters without example") in Pavia
- A two-page document -- one of the very few 15th-century accounts of decrypting ciphertexts
- Written "presumably for use by his collaborators" in the Sforza administration

### Content of the Regulae

The treatise describes how to break **simple substitution ciphers** (NOT nomenclators or polyalphabetic systems):

1. **Language identification**: Determine if ciphertext is Italian or Latin by examining word endings. Italian words end in 5 vowels; Latin has more varied endings.
2. **Vowel detection**: "Ciphers which be at the end of words are ever vowels in the idiom of all Italy."
3. **Frequency analysis**: Identify the most frequent cipher letters; 'e' is most common in Italian.
4. **Pattern recognition**: Analyze two-letter and three-letter word structures; identify geminate letters (likely 'l' or 's').
5. **Obfuscation awareness**: Notes use of nulls ("ciphers representative of no letters") and dual alphabets.

### Were Similar Systems Already in Use?

**Yes, emphatically.** Simonetta's treatise described techniques for breaking ciphers that were "quite outdated at that time." Contemporary cipher systems had already evolved far beyond what his rules could crack:

- The 1401 Mantua cipher already used homophones
- By 1448, the Tranchedini nomenclator had 253 entries
- Simonetta's own cipher department had been operating for decades
- His rules describe frequency analysis that professional cipher clerks had been practicing informally

### His Role

Simonetta was head of the Sforza cipher department in Milan for many years. Almost all ciphertexts that arrived were in Latin or Tuscan. Despite his treatise being historically important as the first written European text on cryptanalysis, it was **already behind the state of the art** when written.

## 7. Surviving 15th-Century Italian Nomenclator Codebooks

### Confirmed Surviving Examples

| Date | Origin | Location | Key Features |
|------|--------|----------|--------------|
| 1401 | Mantua (Gonzaga) | Mantua State Archives | Reversed alphabet + 4 homophonic vowels + nomenclator |
| 1401-1416 | Mantua | Mantua State Archives | 55 cipher keys total for this period |
| 1424 | Florence | Unknown | "Cifra di Galiotto Fibindacci da Ricasoli" |
| 1448 | Milan (Sforza) | Milan State Archives | Tranchedini cipher: 253 signs |
| c. 1450 | Mantua | Mantua State Archives | Expanded nomenclator with syllable groups |
| c. 1450-1455 | Milan | Milan State Archives | 80+ nomenclator entries, 3 homophones/vowel |

### Typical Structure of a 15th-Century Italian Nomenclator

Based on surviving examples, a typical nomenclator codebook contained:

1. **Cipher alphabet**: Each letter of the alphabet mapped to one or more cipher symbols
   - Vowels: 3-4 different symbols each (homophones)
   - Consonants: 1-2 symbols each
   
2. **Nulls**: 4-8 meaningless symbols to be inserted randomly

3. **Doubles**: Special symbols for doubled letters (ll, ss, etc.)

4. **Syllable table**: Arbitrary symbols for common syllable pairs (ab, ac, ad, af, ag, etc.)

5. **Nomenclator proper**: Code symbols for frequently used words:
   - Common words: Come, Quando, Quanto, Non, etc.
   - Names of important people and places
   - Political/diplomatic terms
   
6. **Scale of growth**: By 1450, nomenclators had grown from ~12 word entries to 80+ entries

### Key Observation for Voynich Research

The 15th-century Italian nomenclator was a **designed notation system** that:
- Combined multiple encoding strategies in a single document
- Used invented symbols (not letter-based)
- Was meant to be used with a key/codebook
- Grew in complexity over time
- Was the dominant cipher technology in precisely the time and place the Voynich manuscript was created

## 8. Synthesis: Implications for the Voynich Manuscript

### The Circumstantial Case

The Voynich manuscript (carbon-dated 1404-1438) was created in exactly the time and place where:

1. **Nomenclators were the cutting-edge cipher technology** -- Venice, Mantua, Milan, and Florence were all actively developing and using them
2. **Giovanni Fontana** was creating cipher manuscripts at the University of Padua (1418-1430s) using invented symbol systems
3. **Leon Battista Alberti** was being educated in Venice and Padua (c. 1404-1418), later inventing the cipher disk
4. **Venice's pharmaceutical trade** was enormously valuable and protected by guild secrecy, state licensing, and institutional control
5. **The Council of Ten** was already operating intelligence functions including cryptography (from 1411) and poison/pharmaceutical research
6. **Bartolomeo da Montagnana** was writing pharmaceutical recipe books (Antidotarium) at Padua
7. **No formal cipher systems** for pharmaceutical recipes have been documented -- but the institutional secrecy around theriac and the Council of Ten's pharmaceutical intelligence work show that the MOTIVATION for pharmaceutical encryption existed

### What a "Venetian Pharmaceutical Nomenclator" Would Look Like

If someone in the Padua/Venice circle c. 1420 designed a nomenclator for pharmaceutical content, based on contemporary practice it would likely feature:

- **Invented symbols** not based on any existing alphabet (like Fontana's circles-and-lines system)
- **Multiple symbols for common elements** (homophones for frequent ingredients)
- **A codebook section** mapping symbols to plant names, preparation methods, quantities
- **Nulls and fillers** to obscure patterns
- **Syllable-level encoding** for less common terms
- **Repetitive structure** reflecting the formulaic nature of pharmaceutical recipes

### The Naibbe Cipher (2025 Research)

Michael Greshko's recent peer-reviewed study proposed the "Naibbe cipher" -- a verbose homophonic substitution cipher executable with 15th-century materials (playing cards + dice) that produces ciphertext reproducing many statistical properties of the Voynich manuscript. This demonstrates that the cipher hypothesis remains viable and is consistent with the cryptographic technology available in 15th-century Northern Italy.

### What Remains Unknown

- No direct evidence linking pharmaceutical recipe secrecy to formal cipher use
- No documented cipher manuscripts from the exact Padua medical faculty circle (other than Fontana's)
- No preserved Venetian cipher keys from before 1411
- The gap between Venetian state ciphers (diplomatic) and private/guild cipher use (pharmaceutical) is undocumented
