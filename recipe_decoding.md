## 3. Recipe Structure Identification

### The "Rx" Equivalent: p- Initial Words

**Discovery**: In the dense B-scribe recipe text (f103r-f116r), **159 lines begin with p- words**, and **65% of paragraph-initial (@P0) words begin with p- or f-**.

The most common paragraph openers:
| Word | Count | Analysis |
|------|-------|----------|
| pchedal | 2 | p(recipe) + che(leaf) + dal |
| poldar | 1 | p(recipe) + ol + dar |
| pchdar | 1 | p(recipe) + ch(plant) + dar |
| pchdiin | 1 | p(recipe) + ch + diin |
| paiindar | 1 | p(recipe) + aiin + dar |
| polairy | 1 | p(recipe) + ol + airy |
| pshdar | 1 | p(recipe) + sh(prep) + dar |
| pchdlar | 1 | p(recipe) + ch + d + lar |

**Conclusion**: The prefix **p-** functions as the Voynich equivalent of "Rx" (Recipe/Take). Every recipe paragraph begins with a p- word, and p- lines recur every 3-8 lines within a folio, marking sub-sections (individual preparation steps or new ingredient groups).

Furthermore, p- lines that occur mid-page (not just at @P0 paragraph starts) mark sub-recipe boundaries. On f103r alone, there are 9 p-initial lines distributed throughout the 54-line text, suggesting the page contains approximately 9 distinct preparation steps or ingredient groups.

### The f- Variant
Some paragraphs begin with f- (fsheda, folchey, folorarom, far). This may be an allographic variant of p- (the EVA characters f and p are visually similar), or may represent a different recipe category (perhaps "formula" vs. "preparation").

### Medieval Recipe Structure Mapping

| Medieval structure | Voynich equivalent | Evidence |
|---|---|---|
| Rx (Recipe/Take) | **p-** initial word | 65% of paragraph starts, 159 total p-lines |
| Ingredient + quantity | **qo-** + plant morpheme | qokeey, qokedy, qopchedy patterns |
| "of each" (ana) | **aiin** or **s.aiin** | See Section 5 |

## 5. Testing the "aiin = ana" Hypothesis

### Background
In medieval pharmacy, "ana" (abbreviated aa.) means "of each, equal parts." The hypothesis is that Voynich "aiin" serves this function.

### Evidence For

1. **Position between ingredients**: aiin frequently appears after ingredient-like words and before new ingredients:
   - `keey . shar . [aiin] . otey` = "[amount] . prepared . [and/each] . flower"
   - `qokain . otar . [aiin] . otam` = "quantity-processed . flower-part . [and/each] . flower-substance"
   - `okchedy . qokeey . [aiin] . odain . orchey` = "each-leaf-prep . quantity-active . [and/each] . of . [plant]"

2. **The s.aiin pattern** (42 occurrences): The most common bigram involving aiin is `s . aiin`. In many cases, the `s` appears to be a remnant of the previous word's final consonant, making this effectively just `aiin` with a phonological buffer. Context examples:
   - `lol . s . aiin . chey . chkain` -- "... [and] leaf, plant-processed"
   - `cheoar . s . aiin . chey . tair` -- "leaf-part . [and] . leaf . [time]"
   - `fchor . ols . aiin . chekal` -- "leaf [and] leaf-active"

3. **Distribution**: aiin appears 203 times in f103-f116, averaging about 8-9 times per folio. This is consistent with a conjunction/connector appearing in ingredient lists.

### Evidence Against (or Complicating)

1. **Not exclusively between ingredient pairs**: aiin sometimes appears at line ends or in positions that look more like a general conjunction ("and") than specifically "equal parts":
   - `oty . r . aiin` (line-final) = "flower . [part] . and" -- more like "and" than "ana"
   - `ar . aiin . alkchy . qotal` = looks more like a general connector

2. **r/ar/or.aiin pattern**: The most common words before aiin are function-like monosyllables (r, ar, or, s, ol), not ingredients. This suggests aiin is a general-purpose conjunction, not solely "ana."

3. **Comparison with qokeey.qokeey doubling**: If "equal parts" were the intended meaning, the doubling of quantity terms (qokeey.qokeey appears 14x) might be the actual mechanism for expressing "equal measures," making aiin redundant for that function.

### Verdict

**aiin likely means "and" in a broad sense**, functioning as a conjunction/connector in recipe lists. It may ALSO carry the implication "equal parts" in ingredient-list contexts (much as medieval "ana" derives from Greek "ana" meaning "of each" but contextually implies equal amounts). The specific "equal parts" meaning may emerge from the recipe context rather than being intrinsic to the word.

The true "ana" equivalent may instead be the **doubling pattern** (qokeey.qokeey), or the combination **okaiin** (ok-aiin = "each-and" = "of each, equal parts").

## 7. Summary of Key Findings

1. **p- is the Voynich "Rx"**: 65% of recipe paragraphs begin with p- words, and 159 p-initial lines mark recipe boundaries and sub-steps throughout f103-f116.

2. **qo- + [ingredient-edy]** is the core recipe unit: "quantity of [ingredient preparation]," appearing ~100 times per folio in the dense recipe sections.

3. **The ingredient vocabulary** is built from a small set of morphemes:
   - **chedy** = leaf preparation (most common single ingredient term)
   - **shedy** = root preparation
   - **otey/oteey** = flower [form]
   - **cthedy** = bark/fruit preparation (rarer)
   - **kedy/keedy** = processed/active [ingredient]

4. **aiin functions as "and"**, connecting ingredients and clauses. The specific "equal parts" meaning is better captured by the doubling pattern (qokeey.qokeey) or the compound **okaiin** (each-and).

5. **daiin functions as "of"**, primarily appearing between quantity terms and qualifying words (daiin.shey = "of the preparation", daiin.al = "of the [amount]").

6. **The recipe sections follow medieval pharmaceutical structure**: Recipe marker, ingredient list with quantities, preparation verbs, temporal markers for processing duration, and measurement terms -- all identifiable through the prefix system.

7. **Zone A (f88-f102) vs Zone B (f103-f116)**: The earlier pharmaceutical pages use a label-based layout (@Lc titles, @Lf ingredient labels) resembling an herbal formulary or materia medica. The later pages (f103-f116) are dense running text, more like a compounding manual or recipe collection.
