## PART II: MORPHEME-VISUAL CORRELATION ANALYSIS

### A. Testing -eo- = rounded/whole leaves

Plants containing -eo- in first word:
- **tsheos** (f3r): Elongated striped leaves with THICK ROOTS -- NOT round
- **pcheody** (f16r, from prior analysis): Cannabis -- palmate leaves, NOT round
- **pocheody** (f16r): Palmate, divided (cannabis-type)
- **fchodaiin** (f7r): Palmate, elongated leaflets

**Result**: -eo- does NOT correlate with rounded/whole leaves.
Hypothesis REJECTED.

**Revised hypothesis**: -eo- appears to mark a STATE or CONDITION
(consistent with the grade system where -eo- = state/condition).
In plant codes, -eo- may indicate a plant in a particular developmental
or harvested STATE rather than describing leaf shape.

**Confidence**: LOW for any visual meaning. -eo- appears to be
grammatical (state/condition marker) rather than morphological.

---

### B. Testing -ai- = elongated/linear

Plants containing -ai- in first word:
- **kydainy** (f2r): Deeply DIVIDED palmate leaves -- NOT linear
- **koaiin** (f3v): Jagged/spiny leaves -- NOT linear
- **koair** (f13v): Round/whole paired leaves -- NOT linear
- **pchair** (f47r): Two enormous lobed leaves -- NOT linear
- **paiin** (f10v): Broad drooping whole leaves -- NOT linear
- **psheaiin** (various): Underground emphasis

**Result**: -ai-/-aiin does NOT correlate with elongated/linear leaves.
Hypothesis REJECTED.

**Revised understanding**: -ai-/-aiin is consistently a GRAMMATICAL
morpheme meaning "entity/noun marker" (as per prior statistical analysis).
It marks the word as naming a THING, not describing a visual feature.

**Confidence**: HIGH that -aiin = entity/noun marker (grammatical).

## PART V: SYNTHESIS AND REMAINING UNKNOWNS

### What We Can Now Read

Given a Voynich plant code, we can determine:

1. **Use category** (initial): k- = medicine, t- = process/craft,
   p- = product, f- = aromatic/hot(?)
2. **Plant part emphasis**: ch = leaves/aerial, sh = roots/underground
3. **Leaf morphology**: -yd- = divided/dissected leaves
4. **Specificity**: bare vs +e vs +ee vs +eeo = generic to very specific
5. **Grammatical role**: -y/-aiin/-ol/-dy/-s/-m terminals

### What Remains Unknown

1. **Morpheme for ROUND leaves** -- f2v (kooiin) and f5r (kchody) both
   show round leaves but share no unique morpheme. The -oo- in kooiin
   is a candidate but kchody lacks it.

2. **Morpheme for LINEAR/NARROW leaves** -- f4r (kodalchy) shows linear
   leaves. The -al- might encode "linear/functional form" but -al-
   appears in too many contexts to be purely visual.

3. **Morpheme for SPINY/TOOTHED margins** -- f3v (koaiin), f6r (foar),
   f6v (koarysar) all show spiny plants. No shared unique morpheme
   identified.

4. **Color encoding** -- No morpheme for red, blue, yellow, etc. Colors
   may not be encoded in the naming system (consistent with a system
   that prioritizes FORM over COLOR, as medieval herbalists often did --
   many herbal manuscripts note that color varies and is unreliable for
   identification).

5. **Flower morphology** -- No specific morpheme for petal count, flower
   shape, or flower position identified.

6. **f- prefix** -- Remains unclear. Best hypothesis: aromatic/fragrant
   or fire/hot properties, but only 40% confidence.

### The System's Logic

The Voynich descriptive naming system prioritizes:

1. **USE** (what you DO with the plant) -- first position, highest priority
2. **PART** (which part matters) -- ch aerial vs sh underground
3. **SHAPE** (distinctive morphology) -- -yd- for divided leaves
4. **SPECIFICITY** (how precisely defined) -- grade system

It does NOT prioritize:
- Color (unreliable in dried herbs)
- Flower details (ephemeral, seasonal)
- Size/height (variable with growing conditions)

This is consistent with a PRACTICAL pharmaceutical notation -- the author
cared about: What do I use it for? Which part? How do I recognize it?
How specific am I being?
