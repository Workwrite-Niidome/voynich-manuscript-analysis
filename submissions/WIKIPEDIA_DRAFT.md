### AI-based structural analysis (2026)

In 2026, a computational analysis of the complete EVA transcription (37,779 tokens across 226 folios) was conducted using iterative hypothesis-generation and adversarial hypothesis-destruction cycles, producing over 270 analytical files across 20+ analysis cycles. The study introduced pre-registered blind prediction testing to Voynich scholarship, with predictions recorded before examining manuscript illustrations.[1]

The analysis reported several structural findings that survived cross-validation, permutation testing, and Bonferroni correction. A cross-word suffix dependency was identified, in which the choice among the three most common word-final consonants (n, l, r) is systematically conditioned by the following word's initial character (10-fold cross-validation accuracy 42.9% versus 34.7% baseline, permutation test p < 0.001). This dependency operates within lines but not across line boundaries.[1]

The study characterised a six-layer compositional word structure (prefix, classifier, root, vowel grade, terminal, suffix) with near-zero statistical coupling between prefix and suffix slots (normalised mutual information = 0.032), lower than values measured in natural languages such as Latin, Italian, and Turkish (0.15--0.40).[1]

A correlation was found between vowel complexity in word stems and lexical specificity: words with more complex vowel patterns appear on fewer pages across the manuscript (Pearson r = -0.954). Additionally, 93% of herbal folios begin with words appearing three or fewer times in the herbal section, consistent with the first word functioning as a plant identifier.[1]

The study also reported that 14 of 15 tested morpheme substrings showed non-random distribution across folios after Bonferroni correction for multiple comparisons (6,384 possible substrings tested), and that one morpheme--illustration association survived full-scale validation: the substring "ty" was associated with linear-leaved plants (Fisher exact p = 0.008, odds ratio = 18.0).[1]

The same study documented extensive failures. Of 33 hypotheses tested, 18 were eliminated. Four of five morpheme--feature associations that showed strong results on 30-page training sets failed to replicate when tested on all 112 herbal pages. The hypothesis that the prefix "sh" encodes underground plant parts was refuted by blind prediction testing (25% accuracy). Attempts at phonetic recovery, identification of the text as any known natural language (including Latin, Italian, Arabic, Hebrew, Turkish, and Romani), and classification as a simple substitution or polyalphabetic cipher were all unsuccessful.[1]

The authors concluded that the manuscript was not deciphered but that the validated structural findings were incompatible with simple hoax mechanisms such as a Cardan grille,[2] while remaining consistent with several possible explanations including a constructed notation system, a verbose cipher, or an unidentified natural language.[1]
